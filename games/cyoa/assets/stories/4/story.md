# The Clockwork Carnival

## Keywords

## Chapter 1
### Title
The Return of Thirteen

### Content
You come back to the empty fairground at night, the way you have come back in your mind every day since last winter. The lot is bare—dead grass, rust stains on the concrete where the rides once stood, the smell of old diesel and something sweeter underneath that you can never quite name. Lena disappeared here at exactly 2:17 AM, eight months ago. The townsfolk called it an accident. The police called it a cold case. You call it a lie, because you were supposed to meet her here and you were seventeen minutes late, and a person who knows you best in the world does not simply vanish in seventeen minutes without a reason.

Tonight, the church clock strikes thirteen. There is no thirteenth stroke—there never is—but it happens anyway, one note too many, and brass gates surge from the bare soil as if they were always there waiting just below the surface, curling upright like teeth from a jaw. Calliope music shivers through the fog: bright, mechanical, wrong in some key you can't name but your body recognizes. Steam rolls from nowhere, and a figure steps through it—tall, masked in white porcelain, a coat like black water moving in a breeze that doesn't exist.

The ringmaster holds out two tokens on a gloved palm. One is stamped with a heart, warm to the touch, its edges softly worn. The other is stamped with an hourglass, its glass face catching the carnival lights like a warning. "Choose," he says, and his voice lingers slightly too long in the fog, "what you want more: the person you lost, or the truth about the minute you lost them."

## Chapter 2
### Title
The Mirror Maze of Echoes

### Content
The Mirror Maze smells of salt and old summer—a scent that stops your breath before you have gone three steps. Every panel shows a different memory of Lena. Not distortions, not trick reflections—real moments, precise down to the light and the specific clothes: Lena laughing at fireworks the summer you were both fourteen, head thrown back, unable to stop even after the display ended. Lena on the dock arguing about something that felt enormous then and you cannot now reconstruct. Lena on the night she disappeared, turning back over her shoulder in a way you have replayed so many times the image has worn thin.

The maze is long and the mirrors do not repeat. Each holds something she chose to remember and you chose to remember, the same moment seen through two different lives. By the time you reach the center, you understand that whoever built this place knew Lena well enough to curate her.

At the center stands a locked glass cabinet on a brass pedestal. Inside, on a tarnished cushion: a clockwork compass whose needle spins past north and settles on a direction that does not exist on any standard dial—inward, somewhere below the carnival. A whisper reaches you through the glass, sourceless, faint, close in the way that whispers are close: "She is still here. The engine keeps her." The needle holds steady, pointing toward something deep beneath the carnival's heart. You can follow the compass, or you can leave it sealed and look for answers that don't come from a machine that curates the dead.

### Choices
1. Take the compass and follow the needle toward the engine -> 4
2. Leave the compass and seek a safer path through the carnival -> 5

## Chapter 3
### Title
The Wheel Above Time

### Content
The Ferris wheel is larger than it should be—larger than the lot could contain—its upper gondolas lost in low cloud, the whole structure trembling with the vibration of something vast turning beneath it. The conductor at the boarding gate wears a uniform of deep blue with tarnished brass buttons, and he takes your hourglass token with the practiced ease of someone who has been collecting them for a very long time.

From the summit, the carnival reveals its actual shape. Not rides and tents: gears the size of houses, leather drive belts running beneath the earth at angles that shouldn't be structurally possible, and at the center a single engine pulsing with pale fire, expanding and contracting like a slow breath. You can see what it is from up here in a way you couldn't from the ground: a machine for holding debts. The pale fire is people.

The conductor appears in the seat opposite you—which he was not in a moment ago—and points to a dial on the gondola's interior panel. The dial reads 2:17 AM. "Your sister entered the Wishing Engine of her own will," he says, watching your face. "She traded herself into its ledger to reverse someone else's loss. The machine kept her because the trade was fair." He lets that settle. "If you want her back, the engine is below. Or you can look for someone who knows how to break the machine's rules without standing in front of it."

### Choices
1. Descend to the Wishing Engine and face it directly -> 4
2. Look for someone who knows how to break the machine's rules -> 5

## Chapter 4
### Title
The Wishing Engine

### Content
The Wishing Engine is cathedral-sized and cathedral-loud—a space of brass ribs and tensioned springs and the deep tonal hum of something under enormous pressure holding itself together. The floor vibrates through your boots. The air smells of hot metal and, beneath it, faint and impossible: Lena's shampoo—the lavender kind she kept buying even after the brand changed the formula, because some things you do not stop choosing.

A panel near the base of the central column bears her initials and a timestamp—L.R., 2:17—carved into the brass as though her name was always part of the machine's architecture. You press it and the panel opens. Behind it, a brass scroll unfurls with the terms of a debt: Lena wished on behalf of a stranger, a child who was going to lose something irrecoverable, and the engine accepted the exchange. The wish was honored. Lena knew the cost when she agreed.

The ringmaster is beside you without having walked there. His porcelain mask shows no expression, but his voice is careful—not kind, exactly, but precise. "She is not dead," he says. "She is suspended in the ledger, as she has been since 2:17. She can be released. The engine accepts substitute payment—something of equivalent personal weight." He gives you a moment. "Or you find the clockmaker, who has been seeking a way to reset the entire ledger since before I was installed here. Those are your two doors."

### Choices
1. Pay with your happiest memory to release Lena immediately -> 8
2. Refuse the direct bargain and find the clockmaker's reset method -> 5

## Chapter 5
### Title
The Clockmaker's Terms

### Content
The clockmaker's tent is striped in faded red and gold, lit inside by a single fat candle and a dozen small clocks that do not agree on the time. The man himself is old in a way that makes you think of tools worn smooth by decades of use—hands steady, eyes that move quickly and miss nothing. You tell him about Lena: her disappearance eight months ago, the timestamp 2:17 on the carnival's machinery, the engine you believe is holding her. He listens without interrupting once, then sits quiet long enough that you think he is going to say it cannot be done.

He sets a winding rod in your hand. Brass, double-ended, worn at both grips. "The Wishing Engine has a master spring," he says. "If it is reset correctly before dawn—full sequence, specific order, specific intervals—every unsatisfied debt in the ledger is discharged. Everyone bound inside goes free, including your sister." He meets your eyes across the candlelight. "If the sequence is rushed or forced, the spring fractures. The mechanism fractures. Everyone in the ledger fractures with it." He tells you about the risk so you cannot later say you did not know.

Outside, the sky has started to lighten at the far edge. Dawn is not a metaphor here; it is an actual deadline. You hold the winding rod and feel the weight of his instructions settling in your hands. There is a right way to do this—patient, precise, trusting the sequence—and there is the faster way, the one that does not require patience or trust in someone else's careful engineering.

### Choices
1. Follow the reset sequence exactly at the carousel axle -> 7
2. Ignore the sequence and force the engine to obey you -> 6

## Chapter 6
### Title
Shattered Minute

### Content
You know the sequence. You choose not to use it. The clockmaker's quarter-turns, pauses, and careful intervals feel like someone else's answer to your problem, designed for someone with more time and fewer feelings about the person trapped inside the machine. You jam the winding rod into the gear housing and command the engine to give her back.

The machine screams—not a mechanical sound but something more human, a note of genuine distress from every spring and cog at once. Mirrors crack across the grounds in a rolling wave, glass failing outward from the engine's center. The calliope drops from its bright warped key to something low and final, a chord that presses against your sternum and does not lift. The pale fire inside the engine flickers: the people in the ledger, their debts not discharged but disrupted, no longer held but not freed—torn.

You see Lena for one heartbeat. She is reaching for you through the fracture, mouth open, eyes clear and wide and knowing exactly what you have done. She reaches you as far as the glass held whole, and then the mechanism detonates in a bloom of red sparks and you are on the ground with the winding rod in your fists and nothing left in the machine but heat.

At dawn, the rescue crews find scorched brass scattered across the lot. Your pendant lies in the ash beside a dial still reading 2:17. You are not there to see it. You are nowhere anyone will find, because the machine took you the same way it took everything else—quickly, completely, at the exact moment the spring could not hold.

The End

## Chapter 7
### Title
Dawn Reset

### Content
The carousel axle stands at the center of a ring of horses frozen mid-gallop, their painted eyes directed inward toward the housing where the master spring lives. You are alone here. The clockmaker told you to count the beats between steps, and you count them, and the first quarter-turn goes true and clean, and you pause for the full interval. Full turn. Pause. Lock.

Nothing happens immediately. That is the sequence: you perform the final step and you wait and you do not touch it again, because the clockmaker was absolutely specific about not touching it again, and tonight you have learned that the people inside this machine trusted it with their lives, and you will not be the person who proves that trust was misplaced.

The master spring settles. The sound is quiet—a long, low exhalation, the way an enormous tension sounds when it finally releases. The debt ledgers inside the engine burn to ash in a clean bright flame that produces no smoke. The gates begin to sink back into the earth. The calliope winds down through three descending keys and goes silent.

Lena comes out of the thinning mist at the carnival's eastern edge, cold and shaking and wearing the same coat she had on the night she disappeared. She grabs your hand with both of hers and holds on. She does not speak for a while, and you don't either, because some things need a moment to become true enough to say aloud. The carnival sinks around you, brass folding into soil, and you walk home together through ordinary morning light—the grief still there but different now, the kind that finally has somewhere to rest after eight months of having nowhere to go.

## Chapter 8
### Title
A Memory for a Life

### Content
You press your hand flat on the brass plate beside Lena's panel. The plate is warm, nearly body temperature, and the engine reads you—you can feel it moving through your history like someone turning pages, searching for the right denomination of loss. It finds what it wants. You feel it go: not a subtraction but an erasure, the way a word you have said a thousand times can suddenly become meaningless, except larger, and deeper, and less reversible.

Lena steps free from the panel's housing—breathing, shaking, real. She catches herself on the brass frame and straightens and looks at you, and the tears on her face are steady and unwiped. She tells you about a lake day from childhood, the afternoon you both swore you'd always protect each other: the light on the water, the particular way the hours felt like they would last forever. She tells it the way she always has, with the care and detail of someone who has kept it safe for years.

You smile. You can hear in her voice that it must have been a beautiful day.

She is alive and beside you and the engine has gone quiet and the carnival is already sinking back into the earth around you. You saved her. You kept the promise she is describing, the one made on a sunny afternoon by a lake whose name you cannot quite place. You are glad—wholly, simply glad—that she is standing here.

You just can't remember why the promise mattered so much.

The End
