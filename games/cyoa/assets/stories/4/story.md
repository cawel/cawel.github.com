# The Clockwork Carnival

## Keywords

## Chapter 1
### Title
The Return of Thirteen

### Content
You come back to the empty fairground at night, just as you have come back in your mind every day since last winter. The lot is bare—dead grass, rust stains on the concrete where the rides once stood, the smell of old diesel and something sweeter underneath that you can never quite name. Lena disappeared here at exactly 2:17 AM, eight months ago. The townsfolk called it an accident. The police called it a disappearance with no suspicious circumstances. You called it a lie, because you were supposed to meet her here and you were seventeen minutes late, and the person who knows you best in the world does not simply vanish in seventeen minutes without a reason.

Tonight, the church clock strikes thirteen. There is no thirteenth stroke—there never is—but it strikes again, one note too many, and brass gates surge from the bare soil as if they were always there waiting just below the surface, curling upright like teeth from a jaw. Calliope music shivers through the fog: bright, mechanical, wrong in some key you can't name but your body recognises. Steam rolls from nowhere, and a figure steps through it—tall, masked in white porcelain, a coat like black water moving in a breeze that doesn't exist.

The ringmaster holds out two tokens on a gloved palm. One is stamped with a heart, warm to look at, archaic. One is stamped with an hourglass, its glass face catching the carnival lights in a glint like a warning. "Choose," he says, and his voice resonates slightly too long in the fog, "what you want more: the person you lost, or the truth about the minute you lost them."

## Chapter 2
### Title
The Mirror Maze of Echoes

### Content
The Mirror Maze smells of salt and old summer—a smell that knocks the breath from you before you have gone three steps. Every panel shows a different memory of Lena. Not distortions. Not trick reflections. Real memories, down to the light quality and the specific clothes: Lena laughing at the fireworks the summer you were both teenagers, head tipped back, unable to stop even when the display ended. Lena and you at the dock arguing about something that felt enormous then and you cannot now reconstruct at all. Lena on the night she disappeared, the last moment you have, turning back over her shoulder in a way you have replayed so many times the image has worn thin.

The maze is long and the mirrors do not repeat. Each one is something she chose to keep and you chose to keep, the same moment in two different framings. By the time you reach the centre, you understand that whoever built this place knew her well enough to curate her.

At the centre, a locked glass cabinet stands on a pedestal. Inside it, on a tarnished cushion: a silver key stamped with the time 2:17. A whisper follows you through the glass—sourceless, faint, and close in the way whispers are close: "You can recover her, but never for free." You don't know yet what the price will be. You know the key will open something important. You know that leaving it means choosing a different kind of answer.

### Choices
1. Take the silver key and head for the Wishing Engine -> 4
2. Leave the key and seek a safer path through the carnival -> 5

## Chapter 3
### Title
The Wheel Above Time

### Content
The Ferris wheel is larger than it should be—larger than the lot could contain, its upper gondolas lost in low cloud, the whole structure trembling faintly with the vibration of something enormous turning beneath it. The conductor at the boarding gate wears a uniform of deep blue with tarnished brass buttons, and he takes your hourglass token with the practiced ease of someone who has been collecting them for a long time.

From the summit, the carnival spreads below you in its real shape. Not rides and tents: gears the size of houses, leather drive belts running beneath the earth at angles that shouldn't be structurally possible, and at the centre of everything a single engine pulsing with pale fire, expanding and contracting like a slow breath. You can see what it is from up here in a way you couldn't see from the ground: it is a machine for holding debts. The pale fire is people.

The gondola stops at the top. The conductor appears in the seat opposite you—which he was not in a moment ago—and points to a dial on the gondola's interior panel. The dial is set to 2:17 AM. "Your sibling entered the Wishing Engine of her own will," he says, watching you read that. "She traded herself into its ledger to reverse someone else's loss. She chose freely, so the machine kept her. If you want her back, start with why she went."

1. Pay one year of your future to learn the full ritual -> 4
2. Keep your years and seek another way to free trapped wishers -> 5

## Chapter 4
### Title
The Wishing Engine

### Content
The Wishing Engine is cathedral-sized and cathedral-loud—a space of brass ribs and tensioned springs and the deep tonal hum of something under enormous pressure holding itself together. The floor vibrates through your boots. The air smells of hot metal and, underneath it, faint and impossible: Lena's shampoo.

The silver key fits a panel near the base of the central column, engraved with her initials and the timestamp—L.R., 2:17—as if her name has always been part of the machine's architecture. The panel opens. Behind it, a brass scroll unfurls with the terms of her debt: she wished on behalf of a stranger, a child who was going to lose something irrecoverable, and the Engine accepted the terms and took her in payment. The wish was honoured. Lena knew the cost when she agreed.

The ringmaster is beside you without having walked there. He does not pretend the revelation is easy. "She is not dead," he says, and he means it—you can hear that it is not a comfort he is performing, just a fact he is stating. "She is suspended in the ledger, as she has been since 2:17. She can be released. The Engine accepts substitute payment—something of equivalent personal weight." He gives you a moment with that. "Or you find the clockmaker, who has been looking for a way to reset the whole ledger since before I was installed here. Those are your two doors."

### Choices
1. Pay with your happiest memory to release Lena immediately -> 8
2. Refuse the direct bargain and find the clockmaker's reset method -> 5

## Chapter 5
### Title
The Clockmaker's Terms

### Content
The clockmaker's tent is striped faded red and gold, lit inside by a single fat candle and a dozen small clocks that do not agree on the time. He is old in a way that makes you think of tools worn smooth by long use—hands steady despite everything, eyes that move quickly. He listened to you explain Lena, the debt ledger, the timestamp, without interrupting once. Then he was quiet for long enough that you thought he was going to say it was impossible.

He sets a winding rod in your hand. Brass, double-ended, worn at both grips. "The Wishing Engine has a master spring," he says. "If it is reset correctly before dawn—full sequence, specific order, specific intervals—every unsatisfied debt in the ledger is discharged. Cancelled. Everyone bound inside goes free, including your sibling." He meets your eyes over the candle. "If the sequence is rushed or forced, the spring fractures. The mechanism fractures. Everyone in the ledger fractures with it." He is telling you about the risk so that you understand it is real and so that later you cannot tell yourself you didn't know.

Outside the tent, the sky has started to pale at the far edge. Dawn is not a metaphor here; it is an actual deadline. You hold the winding rod and feel the weight of the clockmaker's instructions settling in your hands.

### Choices
1. Follow the reset sequence exactly at the carousel axle -> 7
2. Ignore the sequence and force the engine to obey you -> 6

## Chapter 6
### Title
Shattered Minute

### Content
You know the sequence. You choose not to use it. The clockmaker's quarters, full turns, and careful pauses feel like someone else's answer to your problem, designed for someone with more time and fewer feelings. You jam the winding rod into the gear housing and command the engine to give her back.

The machine screams—not a mechanical failure sound but something more human, a note of genuine distress from every spring and cog at once. Mirrors crack across the grounds in a rolling wave, glass failing outward from the engine's centre. The calliope drops from its bright warped key to something low and final, a note that sits on your sternum. The pale fire inside the engine flickers: the people in the ledger, their debts not discharged but disrupted, no longer held but not freed—torn.

You see Lena for one heartbeat. She is reaching for you through the fracture, mouth open, eyes clear. She reaches you exactly as far as the glass was whole, and then the mechanism detonates in a bloom of red sparks and you are on the ground with the winding rod in your hands and nothing in the machine but heat.

At dawn, rescuers find scorched brass and your pendant in the ash. The minute 2:17 is still on the carnival's dial. You are not there to read it.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 7
### Title
Dawn Reset

### Content
The carousel axle is at the centre of a ring of horses frozen mid-gallop, their painted eyes directed inward, toward the housing where the master spring lives. You are alone here. The clockmaker told you to count the beats between steps, and you count them, and the first quarter-turn goes true and clean, and you pause for the full count. Full turn. Pause. Lock.

Nothing happens immediately. That is the sequence: you do the final step and you wait and you do not touch it again, because the clockmaker was very specific about not touching it again, and you have understood tonight that the people in this machine trusted the machine and you will not be the person who proves that trust was misplaced.

The master spring settles. The sound is not dramatic—a long, low exhalation, the way a very tense thing sounds when it finally releases. The debt ledgers inside the engine burn to ash in a clean bright flame that produces no smoke. The gates begin to sink back into the earth. The calliope winds down through three keys and goes quiet.

Lena comes out of the thinning mist at the carnival's east edge, cold and shaken and wearing the same coat she had on the night she disappeared. She grabs your hand with both of hers and holds on. She does not say anything for a while, and you don't either, because some things take a moment to be true enough to speak. You walk home through ordinary morning light, the grief still present but different now—the kind that has somewhere to rest, finally, after eight months of having nowhere to go.

## Chapter 8
### Title
A Memory for a Life

### Content
You press your hand flat on the brass plate beside Lena's panel. The plate is warm, almost body temperature, and the engine reads you—you can feel it moving through your history like someone turning pages, searching for the right denomination of loss. It finds what it wants. You feel it go: not a subtraction but an erasure, the way a word you've said a thousand times can suddenly feel meaningless, except larger.

Lena steps free from the panel's housing—breathing, shaking, real. She catches herself on the brass frame and then straightens and looks at you, and there are tears on her face that she doesn't bother to address. She tells you about a lake day from your childhood, the one where you both swore you'd always protect each other, the light on the water, the specific way the afternoon felt like it would last. She tells it the way she always has—with the detail of someone who has kept it carefully.

You smile. You can hear in her voice that it was a beautiful day.

She is alive and beside you and the engine has gone quiet and the carnival is already beginning to sink back into the earth around you. You saved her. You kept the promise she is describing. You are glad, with every part of yourself, that she is here.

You just can't remember making it.

The End
