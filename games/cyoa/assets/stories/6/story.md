# Echoes of the Perseid Gate

## Keywords
- spaceship
- anomaly

## Chapter 1
### Title
Arrival at Perseid

### Content
You command the research vessel Asterion on a high-risk assignment: assess the Perseid Gate before the flare window closes in six weeks. Outside the bridge glass, the ring hangs like a broken star—roughly three kilometers across, older than any human record, and humming at a frequency you feel in your back teeth before it registers on sensors. Its surface is pitted alloy of no known composition, etched with channel lines that glow faintly in the magnetar light.

Its unstable harmonics now ripple near inhabited shipping routes. Two freighters have already been lost to micro-shear events in the approach corridor—torn apart in ways that left debris fields the salvage teams couldn't fully account for. Command's order is clear: determine whether the gate can be stabilized or must be destroyed before civilian traffic season opens.

You keep your eyes on the ring. Navigation officer Rhea has the approach vectors ready. Getting closer will give you real data fast, but passive analysis from a safe standoff might reveal what you're actually dealing with before you put the hull at risk.

2. Hold distance and run passive analysis first -> 3

## Chapter 2
### Title
Shear Zone

### Content
At close range, Asterion phase-skips in microbursts—your instruments briefly show the ship occupying two positions at once, and the hull groans each time the readings converge. Navigation officer Rhea speaks without lifting her eyes from the console: "Three more cycles of this and I'm filing a protest with my union. Four more and there won't be a union to file it with."

The data stream is extraordinary. You're pulling enough harmonic information to identify a transit corridor running through the gate's centerline—a clear lane through the instability—and the feed reveals what look like manual control pylons anchored to the inner ring structure. None of that was in the pre-mission briefing. Someone built this to be operated from the inside.

Rhea calls the shear risk again, sharper this time. Your astrophysicist Kenji confirms the readings are degrading the hull's phase coherence faster than the shielding can compensate. Whatever is in there, you're learning fast—but the ship has a limit, and you are approaching it.

### Choices
1. Send an unmanned probe through the corridor -> 4
2. Back off and integrate data with passive analysis -> 3

## Chapter 3
### Title
Lock Rhythm

### Content
At safe distance, astrophysicist Kenji spends forty minutes with the harmonic data before he emerges from his analysis station looking like someone who has solved a problem he expected to take days. The gate is not randomly unstable. It pulses on an eleven-minute cycle tied to the local magnetar wind, and transit is only genuinely safe during the narrow intervals when the pulse locks into phase.

"Outside those windows, matter doesn't fragment cleanly," Kenji says. "It fragments in ways that are difficult to describe to next of kin." He has identified one viable transit window: thirty-seven minutes from now. After that, the next safe window is seven hours out and the flare forecast deteriorates sharply.

The transit window could carry a crewed vessel through for a direct interior inspection of the gate structure. Alternatively, sending an unmanned probe through on the same window would keep the crew safe while still gathering data from the far side—whatever is on the other end of this ring.

### Choices
1. Use the window for a crewed transit to inspect from inside -> 5
2. Send an unmanned probe through the transit window -> 4

## Chapter 4
### Title
Probe Debrief

### Content
The probe returns twelve minutes after transit, its hull coated in fine crystal dust that was not there when it left. Its environmental sensors recorded temperatures, pressures, and radiation profiles that do not match any catalogued space environment—and embedded in its memory banks is what appears to be an automated maintenance warning, written in a script that takes Kenji another twenty minutes to partially decode.

"Lattice breach progressing," he reads aloud, the words careful. "Calibrate or collapse." The gate is failing machinery with a maintenance log and an active fault condition, not a random anomaly. Someone built this to be serviced, and the service is overdue.

The star maps in the probe's memory show connected systems not on any current chart. There is more on the other side of that ring than debris and silence. The question is whether you attempt to fix the machine—using the control pylons the probe confirmed on the inner ring—or shut the gate down permanently before it fails on its own terms and takes the approach corridor with it.

### Choices
1. Attempt live calibration at the control pylons -> 6
2. Prepare controlled demolition charges for full closure -> 7

## Chapter 5
### Title
Dead System Beacon

### Content
You transit through the lock interval and emerge into a stillness so complete it registers as wrong on every instrument. The linked system is derelict—no active ships, no radio traffic, no light from any settlement. Whatever civilization built the gate, they are not here anymore. The only thing still running is a maintenance AI: its signal patient and faint, broadcasting on a frequency that suggests it has been waiting a very long time for someone qualified to answer.

The AI requests emergency lattice repair and confirms what your approach data suggested: if the breach progresses unchecked, cascade failure will propagate back through the gate and destabilize your own system's approach corridors. It provides calibration specifications. It also confirms that full closure is possible—but irreversible. Once collapsed, the connection cannot be rebuilt with any technology it knows of.

You are standing in someone else's abandoned infrastructure, and it is asking you to decide its fate. The control pylons are accessible from here—exposed along the inner ring scaffold, each one fitted with manual override interfaces. Alternatively, your demolition ordnance can reach the ring's primary stress points from this position.

### Choices
1. Trust the AI guidance and attempt calibration -> 6
2. Return to Asterion's position and execute closure protocol -> 7

## Chapter 6
### Title
Three Overrides

### Content
Calibration requires three synchronized manual overrides on exposed pylons embedded in the inner ring scaffold. The pylons are reachable—barely—but each one sits inside a radiation corridor that spikes unpredictably as the lattice fault cycles. Your suit shielding will handle two exposures with acceptable risk. A third pushes the exposure math into territory the manufacturer did not design for.

Rhea has already run the timing calculations. A full team executing all three overrides simultaneously has the best completion odds but distributes radiation risk across everyone on the bridge crew. Sending a single runner in sequence is faster and protects the rest—but the accumulated dose to whoever runs it will almost certainly be fatal before recovery drones can reach them.

Nobody speaks, but the bridge is very quiet. You can feel the weight of their waiting. Kenji's hands rest flat on his console. Rhea's eyes are on you, and they are steady, and they are not going to make this easier by looking away. You tell them what you have decided.

### Choices
1. Execute full-team synchronized override -> 10
2. Take the runner baton yourself to finish in time -> 8

## Chapter 7
### Title
Closure Clock

### Content
The demolition charges are armed and aligned on the ring's primary stress points. A clean detonation will collapse the gate into inert fragments in under four seconds. Cascade risk ends. The anomaly is resolved. The two linked systems will be permanently severed, and whatever lies beyond—those unmapped systems, the silent civilization's remaining traces, anything the maintenance AI was built to protect—becomes permanently inaccessible.

Rhea runs the detonation checklist without being asked. Kenji stands at his station and says nothing, which is how you know he is uncomfortable with the decision. He mapped the lock rhythm to keep options open. He will not tell you that directly, but you know it anyway.

The timer reaches sixty seconds and crosses the no-return threshold. After this point, the detonation sequence cannot be aborted without risking a partial collapse—worse than either full closure or full calibration. You confirm now, or you abort and commit to the pylons.

### Choices
1. Confirm detonation and seal the horizon -> 9
2. Abort closure and return to calibration plan -> 6

## Chapter 8
### Title
One Runner

### Content
You take the runner baton before anyone can argue—partly because the decision is yours to own, partly because you know Rhea will volunteer if you hesitate even a moment longer. The first pylon override takes forty seconds and costs you one suit exposure. The second takes longer because the scaffold shifts mid-approach and you have to find a new handhold in the dark, your gloves slipping on crystal dust before they catch. Your dosimeter crosses the safe threshold somewhere during the climb back.

The third override is at the far end of the pylon bridge and the radiation bloom hits when you are halfway across, a wall of invisible heat that your instruments translate into numbers you do not look at. You complete the final connection by feel because your visor display has gone to static. The lattice rethreads. The gate's hum changes pitch—lower, steadier, resolving into something that sounds almost like relief—and holds.

Your telemetry flatlines before the recovery drones reach you. The gate stabilizes and remains stable. Cascade risk drops to zero. When Kenji files his mission report, he writes your name at the top and spends three paragraphs describing what you did in language more careful than anything he has ever written. He does not know what else to do with it.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 9
### Title
Sealed Horizon

### Content
You confirm detonation. The charges fire in the sequence Rhea programmed and the ring fractures outward in a silent bloom of silver debris that scatters across the approach corridor and slowly stills. Local space settles. Every anomalous harmonic reading drops to zero within ninety seconds. The magnetar wind flows clean through the space where the gate stood, as though nothing was ever there.

Command praises the decision when you report in: safe, decisive, irreversible. The debrief is thorough and the commendation is genuine. You kept your entire crew alive and eliminated a demonstrable threat to inhabited routes. That is not a small thing. Billions of people on civilian shipping lanes are safer today than yesterday.

Kenji submits a supplemental note with his mission data, flagging the unmapped star systems in the probe's telemetry because he believes someone should know they existed. You sign the note without altering a word. The door is sealed. It cannot be reopened. You think about that sometimes—in the quiet watches when the bridge is dim and the stars outside look exactly like they did before you learned there were more of them. Not with regret, exactly. With the particular weight of a question that no longer has a place to go.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 10
### Title
Gatekeepers of Dawn

### Content
Rhea takes pylon one, Kenji takes pylon two, and you take three. The overrides execute simultaneously—three people in three radiation corridors, counting the same beats, trusting the same timing data. The bloom comes in the middle of the sequence, as Kenji's models predicted, and it is worse than the models suggested. You finish the connection through it anyway, because stopping partway through is not a calibration—it is just damage with extra steps.

All three overrides complete within four seconds of each other. The lattice rethreads into stable geometry across the entire ring structure. The gate's hum drops from its dangerous harmonic into something steady and low, the sound of a machine doing what it was built to do. The maintenance AI certifies negligible cascade risk and transmits a final log entry before going quiet—the first transmission it has completed in what its timestamp suggests is several hundred years. Rhea has a cracked rib from the scaffold impact. Kenji's left hand will need surgery. You are not unscathed either.

Command establishes a multinational oversight charter using your calibration data as its technical foundation. The Perseid Gate becomes the most studied structure in human space. Years later, cadets work through your mission logs—not as a story about heroism, but as evidence that exploration and responsibility travel together, or do not travel at all.

### Choices
The End
