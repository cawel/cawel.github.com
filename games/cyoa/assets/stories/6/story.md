# Echoes of the Perseid Gate

## Keywords
- spaceship
- anomaly
- gate

## Chapter 1
### Title
Arrival at Perseid

### Content
You command the research vessel Asterion on a high-risk mission: assess the Perseid Gate before the flare window closes. Outside the bridge glass, the ring hangs like a broken star—roughly three kilometers across, older than any human record, and humming at a frequency you feel in your back teeth before you hear it on sensors.

Its unstable harmonics now ripple near inhabited routes. Two freighters have already been lost to micro-shear events in the approach corridor. Command's order is clear: determine whether it can be stabilized or must be destroyed before civilian traffic season opens in six weeks.

You keep your eyes on the ring. Getting closer will give you real data fast, but passive analysis from a safe standoff might first reveal what you're actually dealing with.

### Choices
1. Begin close-range harmonic mapping immediately -> 2
2. Hold distance and run passive analysis first -> 3

## Chapter 2
### Title
Shear Zone

### Content
At close range, Asterion phase-skips in microbursts—your instruments briefly show the ship occupying two positions simultaneously, and the hull groans each time. Navigation officer Rhea speaks without taking her eyes from her console: "Three more cycles of this and I'm filing a protest with my union. Four more and there won't be a union to file it with."

The data stream is extraordinary. You're pulling enough harmonic data to identify a transit corridor running through the gate's centerline, and the feed shows what look like manual control pylons anchored to the inner ring structure. None of that was in the pre-mission briefing.

Rhea calls the shear risk again, sharper this time. Whatever is in there, you're learning fast—but the hull has a limit.

### Choices
1. Send an unmanned probe through the corridor -> 4
2. Back off and integrate with passive analysis -> 3

## Chapter 3
### Title
Lock Rhythm

### Content
At safe distance, astrophysicist Kenji spends forty minutes with the harmonic data before he emerges from his station looking like someone who has solved a puzzle he expected to take days. The gate is not randomly unstable. It pulses on an eleven-minute cycle tied to the local magnetar wind, and transit is only genuinely safe during the narrow intervals when the pulse locks.

"Outside those windows, matter doesn't fragment cleanly," Kenji says. "It fragments in ways that are difficult to describe to next of kin." He has identified one viable transit window: thirty-seven minutes from now. After that, the next window is seven hours out and the flare forecast turns hostile.

You have time to use the window for a crewed transit to inspect the structure from inside, or to skip transit entirely and send Command your recommendation before the situation deteriorates.

### Choices
1. Use the window for a crewed transit to inspect from inside -> 5
2. Skip transit and transmit closure recommendation to command -> 9

## Chapter 4
### Title
Probe Debrief

### Content
The probe returns twelve minutes after transit, its hull coated in fine crystal dust that wasn't there when it left. Its environmental sensors recorded temperatures, pressures, and radiation profiles that don't match any catalogued space environment—and embedded in its memory is what appears to be an automated maintenance warning, written in a script that takes Kenji another twenty minutes to partially decode.

"Lattice breach progressing," he reads. "Calibrate or collapse." The gate is failing machinery with a maintenance log and an active fault condition, not a random anomaly. Someone built this to be serviced.

The star maps in the probe's memory show connected systems not on any current chart. There is more on the other side of that ring than debris. The question is whether to try to fix the machine or shut it down before it fails on its own.

### Choices
1. Attempt live calibration at the control pylons -> 6
2. Prepare controlled demolition charges for full closure -> 7

## Chapter 5
### Title
Dead System Beacon

### Content
You transit through the lock interval and emerge in a stillness so complete it registers as wrong on every instrument. The linked system is derelict—no active ships, no radio traffic, no light from any settlement. Whatever civilization built the gate, they are not here anymore. The only thing running is a maintenance AI, its signal patient and faint, broadcasting on a frequency that suggests it has been waiting a very long time for someone qualified to answer.

It requests emergency lattice repair and confirms what the probe's data suggested: if the breach progresses unchecked, cascade failure will propagate back through the gate and destabilize your own system too. It provides calibration geometry. It also confirms that full closure is possible, but irreversible—once collapsed, the connection cannot be rebuilt with any technology it knows of.

You are standing in someone else's abandoned infrastructure, and it is asking you to decide its fate.

### Choices
1. Trust the AI guidance and attempt calibration -> 6
2. Return immediately and execute closure protocol -> 7

## Chapter 6
### Title
Three Overrides

### Content
Calibration requires three synchronized manual overrides on exposed pylons embedded in the inner ring scaffold. The pylons are reachable—barely—but each one sits inside a radiation corridor that spikes unpredictably as the lattice fault cycles. Your suit will handle two exposures with acceptable risk. A third pushes the math into territory the manufacturer did not design for.

Rhea has already done the timing calculation. A full team running all three simultaneously has the best completion odds but distributes risk across everyone. Sending a single runner in sequence is faster and protects the crew—but the accumulated exposure to whoever runs it will almost certainly be fatal before recovery drones can reach them.

Nobody speaks, but the bridge is very quiet. You tell them what you've decided.

### Choices
1. Execute full-team synchronized override -> 10
2. Take the runner baton yourself to finish in time -> 8

## Chapter 7
### Title
Closure Clock

### Content
The charges are armed and aligned on the ring's primary stress points. A clean detonation will collapse the gate into inert fragments in under four seconds. Cascade risk ends. The anomaly is resolved. The two linked systems will be permanently isolated from each other, and whatever lies beyond the gate—those unmapped systems, the silent civilization's remaining traces, anything the maintenance AI was guarding—becomes permanently inaccessible.

Rhea runs the detonation checklist without being asked. Kenji stands at his station and says nothing, which is how you know he's uncomfortable with the call. He mapped the lock rhythm to keep options open. He won't tell you that, but you know it anyway.

The timer is at sixty seconds and crossing no-return. You confirm the decision now, or you abort and go back to the pylons.

### Choices
1. Confirm detonation and seal the horizon -> 9
2. Abort closure and return to calibration plan -> 6

## Chapter 8
### Title
One Runner

### Content
You take the runner baton before anyone can argue, partly because the decision is yours to own and partly because you know Rhea will volunteer if you hesitate. The first pylon override takes forty seconds and costs you one suit exposure. The second takes longer because the scaffold shifts mid-approach and you have to find a new handhold in the dark. Your dosimeter crosses the safe threshold somewhere during the climb back.

The third override is at the far end of the pylon bridge and the radiation bloom hits when you are halfway across. You complete the final connection by feel because your visor display has gone to static. The lattice rethreads. The gate's hum changes pitch—lower, steadier—and holds.

Your telemetry flatlines before the recovery drones reach you. The gate stabilizes and remains stable. Cascade risk is gone. When Kenji files his mission report, he writes your name at the top and spends three paragraphs describing what you did. He doesn't know what else to do with it.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 9
### Title
Sealed Horizon

### Content
You confirm detonation. The charges fire in the sequence Rhea set and the ring fractures outward in a silent bloom of silver debris that scatters across the approach corridor and slowly stills. Local space settles. Every anomalous harmonic reading drops to zero within ninety seconds. Command praises the decision when you report in: safe, decisive, irreversible.

The debrief is thorough and the commendation is genuine. You kept your entire crew alive and eliminated a demonstrable threat to inhabited routes. That is not a small thing. Billions of people on civilian shipping lanes are safer today than they were yesterday.

Kenji submits a supplemental note with his mission data, flagging the unmapped star systems in the probe's memory because he feels someone should know they existed. You sign the note without changing anything. The door is sealed. It cannot be reopened. You think about that sometimes, in the way you think about doors.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 10
### Title
Gatekeepers of Dawn

### Content
Rhea takes pylon one, Kenji takes pylon two, and you take three. The overrides run simultaneously. The radiation bloom comes in the middle of the sequence, as predicted, and it is worse than the model suggested. You finish the connection through it anyway, because stopping partway is not a calibration—it is just damage.

All three overrides complete within four seconds of each other. The lattice rethreads into stable geometry across the entire ring structure. The maintenance AI certifies negligible cascade risk and transmits a final log entry—the first it has been able to send in what its timestamp suggests is several hundred years—before going quiet. Rhea has a cracked rib. Kenji's left hand will need surgery. You are not unscathed either.

Command establishes a multinational oversight charter using your calibration data as the technical foundation. The gate becomes the most studied structure in human space. Years later, cadets still work through your mission logs in detail—not as a story about heroism, but as proof that exploration and responsibility are not opposites. They travel together, or they do not travel at all.

### Choices
The End
