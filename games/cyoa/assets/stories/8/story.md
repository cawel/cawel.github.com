# Crown of the Underforge

## Keywords
- wizards
- dwarves
- dungeons

## Chapter 1
### Title
The Briefing Before Descent

### Content
You descend toward the Underforge with a joint writ sealed by both the surface council and the dwarven clans, the parchment still warm from the stamping dies. The writ is a piece of paper trying to do the work of trust, and everyone who signed it knows it. Heat rises through the stone steps in pulses, carrying the smell of sulphur and iron and something older beneath both—the slow exhalation of the mountain's interior, breathing in its own time.

Your mission is explicit. Prevent civil war. Stabilize the failing forge-heart before it destabilizes the magma channels holding the whole lower structure together. Keep the Crown of the Underforge from becoming a weapon—which means keeping it from becoming a prize. The crown does not command armies; it amplifies oaths. In the right hands that is an instrument of governance. In the wrong hands it is a very loud way to start a fire.

Clan-Mother Brynja is your named ally below, the one figure with enough standing in both dwarven and wizard factions to hold a coalition together. You need to reach her before rebel cells and rogue mages decide the vault is easier to take than to negotiate. You have a choice about how you enter.

### Choices
1. Seek Brynja in the Oathhall and enter with lawful support -> 2
2. Use abandoned wizard tunnels to reach the crown first -> 3

## Chapter 2
### Title
Oathhall Alliance

### Content
The Oathhall is older than the current crisis—its banners scarred black along the edges from recent sabotage, the smoke still faint in the cold rafters. Brynja meets you beneath them with two clan-guards and no ceremony. She is shorter than you expected, broader, with the particular stillness of someone who has held command in emergencies often enough that urgency no longer shows on her face. She reads the writ without expression and sets it on the table.

She confirms what the surface council feared but could not prove: two binding pylons in the outer ring are failing simultaneously, and each faction is accusing the other of deliberate sabotage while the magma wyrm—a creature living in the mountain's deep channels, normally dormant—stirs at the disruption. When it answers instability with movement, the iron bridges flex and the foundries flood. She has been holding armed factions apart through protocol alone for three days. Protocol is nearly spent.

She will back you, she says, with full clan legitimacy—access, escort, authority to speak in her name at the vault. Her condition: shared command. No unilateral decisions about the crown. You understand what she is offering—and what she is asking you to give up.

### Choices
1. Accept Brynja's oversight and proceed to the crown vault together -> 6
2. Volunteer to stabilize the pylons first before touching the crown -> 5

## Chapter 3
### Title
Wizard Tunnels

### Content
The abandoned wizard routes run parallel to the main corridors, narrow and low and smelling of dead spellwork—the particular chalky dryness of old wards that have not been renewed and are quietly failing. Someone has been through here recently; fresh scratch-marks on the walls mark junction points in a cipher you half-recognise from rebel communiqués you read in the briefing. They were here within the last day.

You move fast through fallen archways and over cracked floor-seals, covering the distance to the lower levels in half the time the main route would cost. Then the third ward you step across—intact, unlike the rest—flares under your boot and a resonance tone rolls outward through the stone in every direction. You cannot unring it. Every faction with ears in the mountain now knows someone is moving through the old wizard routes, and the only things that move through the old wizard routes are people who don't want to be announced.

A dwarven scout, young, pinned behind a collapsed arch with a twisted ankle, watches you crouch beside her with the particular expression of someone weighing options quickly. She tells you: Brynja is holding the Oathhall with clan guard and is genuinely trying to stabilize things. She will help you reach the vault—but she has heard the resonance alarm, and if you arrive looking like another faction racing for the crown, she may oppose you instead. You have the choice of which you look like.

### Choices
1. Push onward and seize the crown before anyone else -> 6
2. Follow the sabotage traces in these tunnels to the archive chamber first -> 4

## Chapter 4
### Title
Ash Ledger

### Content
The archive chamber is ankle-deep in ash and scattered documents—someone was here to destroy something in a hurry and did not quite finish. The surviving pages are scorched at the edges and fragile, and you move through them with the careful hands of someone who understands that evidence has a habit of being the first casualty of power struggles.

What you piece together takes twenty minutes and costs you urgency you cannot entirely afford. But the picture is complete: both the dwarven hardliners and the wizard faction's inner circle tampered with the same two pylons, at different times, in coordinated sequence—an arrangement made in secret, timed to force a crisis that would make the crown appear to be the only solution. The civil war everyone is trying to prevent was manufactured. The people most loudly demanding the crown as the answer are the ones who ensured it would be needed.

You have evidence that could shatter both factions' authority and create space for something more legitimate in the gap. It is also the kind of evidence that makes you very valuable to eliminate before you can present it. The mountain is still shaking. The wyrm has not gone back to sleep.

### Choices
1. Take the evidence to Brynja immediately -> 2
2. Keep it hidden and use it as leverage when you reach the vault -> 6

## Chapter 5
### Title
Pylon Chambers

### Content
The outer pylon ring is a ring in name only—in practice, a series of chambers carved into the mountain's mid-layer, each one housing a column of forged iron and dwarven runework that channels the forge-heart's energy into a stable pressure gradient. Without them the internal heat does not distribute evenly. Without even distribution, the deep channels overpressure. When the deep channels overpressure, the magma wyrm wakes up annoyed.

You and Brynja's engineers reach the first chamber as fresh magma vents crack open in the floor with a sound like splitting teeth. The first pylon resets cleanly—Brynja's lead engineer knows her work, hands steady, speaking her commands in the flat tone of someone who reserves emotion for off-shift hours. Two pylons remain failing. The second chamber is already running hot, the runework oxidising visibly on the column's surface.

The wizard envoy who appears at the chamber entrance offers volatile spellfire—accelerant magic that can force a rapid stabilisation at the cost of structural finesse, available now, while time remains. Brynja does not discuss it; she tells you controlled forging is the only approach that doesn't risk catastrophic rebound. The disagreement between them is genuine, not political: two people who both know what they're talking about, reaching different conclusions. From the lower channels, the wyrm roars, and molten iron weeps from a ceiling joint.

### Choices
1. Use volatile spellfire to force a rapid stabilization -> 7
2. Fall back to the crown vault for the harmonizing rite -> 6

## Chapter 6
### Title
The Crown Vault

### Content
The crown vault is not a room—it is a judgement. The ceiling vaults fifty feet above a lake of living iron, its surface slow and bright as thought, and the Crown of the Underforge hangs at the centre anchored to three oath pillars by chains of dwarven alloy, each link engraved with a covenant clause in a script older than the current clans. The heat is significant. The silence is more so.

Brynja arrives beside you with clan guard at her shoulder. Wizard envoys fill the upper gallery, robes making soft sounds in the vault's rising air. Every faction that has been racing toward this moment has arrived at it simultaneously, which means no one has won the approach and everyone is watching everyone else reach for something none of them will say they want.

You know what the crown actually does—not from the briefing, but from the ash ledger, or from the Oathhall, or from the simple fact of what you have watched factions do to each other on the way here: the crown does not command the wyrm alone. It amplifies whatever oath it is bound to. Bound to one person it amplifies that person's will across every forge-bonded channel in the mountain. One hand on the crown means everyone else's hand off the mountain. But the oath pillars are plural. Three pillars. Three seats. The architecture of the thing was never built for a single bearer.

### Choices
1. Claim the crown for yourself and impose order by force -> 7
2. Bind the crown to a shared council oath with Brynja and the wizard envoy -> 9
3. Seal the vault and deny the crown to everyone -> 8

## Chapter 7
### Title
Fire Without Consent

### Content
You take the crown from its chains—the metal cold despite the lake beneath it, cold in the way that power is sometimes cold: waiting, indifferent to who holds it—and you press it to your brow and issue the first command. Both factions kneel. Not willingly; the amplification does not distinguish between willing compliance and its absence. That is the first thing you learn.

The pyrlon lattice begins to fail within minutes. The crown amplifies your oath, and your oath was order, but order imposed by coercion is not stability—it is pressure with nowhere to go. The mountain reads it the same way it reads any overpressured channel: as an emergency requiring release. The magma wyrm does not serve tyrants. It answers domination with the only language it has ever known.

Iron bridges collapse in sequence, the sound traveling from the deep channels upward like a wave. Foundries flood. The factions that knelt are running now, in opposite directions, which is the first choice they have made freely since you took the crown. You hold it until the last bridge gives way beneath you, and the living iron lake rises, and the Underforge burns under your name—remembered, after, as the person who came to prevent a war and started one alone.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 8
### Title
Stone Silence

### Content
You find the vault's sealing mechanism behind the third oath pillar—a sequence of recessed runestones the original architects built as a last resort, for the scenario where the crown could not be safely used and could not be safely destroyed. You work the sequence. The vault answers: stone grinds against stone in a deep chord, the chains release and retract, and the Crown of the Underforge descends into the living iron lake and does not resurface.

The war halts. Not because anyone chose peace—because the object everyone was willing to start the war over no longer exists to be claimed. The factions withdraw to their positions and look at each other across the silence of the vault's sealed doors with the particular expression of people who have been mid-argument when the subject of the argument was removed. It is not resolution. It is the absence of a conclusion.

The forge-heart remains unstable without the harmonizing rite, the pylons holding at reduced capacity, the mountain running always a degree hotter than is comfortable and never quite returning to full production. Rationing is the word that defines the next three generations. The mountain survives—scarred and cautious, producing enough, never flourishing. You are remembered as the one who chose survival over renewal, which is not an insult but is not entirely a compliment either. It is simply what you chose, and the mountain lives it out in your absence for a long time afterward.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 9
### Title
The New Accord

### Content
You place your hand on the first oath pillar and require Brynja and the wizard envoy to do the same. The word "require" is exactly right: you do not ask, but you also do not command. You simply do not move until they join you. Brynja steps forward first, one hand flat on the cold pillar, her guards watching her read you and choose. The envoy follows—not easily, not without looking at his own faction in the upper gallery and making a visible decision—and then all three of you are touching the same stone.

The crown's binding terms are spoken aloud: a three-seat covenant, no single bearer, no unilateral command over the forge-heart's channels, and an automatic dissolution clause if any seat violates the charter. The crown descends from its chains of its own accord and settles into the centre of the three pillars, suspended in the oath rather than the chains. The living iron lake stills. The harmonizing rite follows—Brynja's engineers at the pylons, the wizard envoy calibrating the upper resonance array, and the crown functioning as the instrument it was built to be: not a throne, not a weapon, an amplifier of something that requires three voices to produce.

The wyrm goes quiet in the deep channels. By nightfall, anvils ring again in the upper foundries—not the frantic sound of crisis or the hollow sound of rationing, but the good hard rhythm of work resuming. Underforge enters a harder era than the old one, an era where power must be negotiated in every session and no faction can simply outweigh the others. It is slower. It is frustrating. Several factions call it a defeat. The mountain's people, the ones who work the forges and walk the iron bridges, mostly call it something other than war, which turns out to be enough.

### Choices
The End
