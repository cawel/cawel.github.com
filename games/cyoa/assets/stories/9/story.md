# The Fracture Hour

## Chapter 1
### Title
The Alarm You've Heard Before

### Content
You jolt awake on the same bunk, third ring, with copper flooding your mouth and the station alarm cycling red overhead. 04:07 ship-time. Varda Station, asteroid belt. Your jump-suit is draped over the chair in the same arc it always falls. The half-eaten ration bar is on the shelf, two bites from the end, exactly where you left it—not you, strictly speaking, but the you who was here yesterday and every yesterday before that.

You have been here before. This specific morning, this precise second. More times than you have stopped counting.

In two hours and forty minutes, a Fracture cluster—an alien swarm the survey logs can't classify, something angular and dark and fast—will punch through the observation deck's outer pane and kill seventeen people. The first loop you died in the breach itself, thrashing inside a suit whose seal had burned in the initial strike. After that you started waking up here, always this same red alarm, always with every previous loop intact in your memory like scars that don't quite fade.

The artifact in the station's sealed lower vault is the anchor. Dara in engineering has been getting closer to reading it. You are running out of patience and nearly out of new approaches. The Fracture comes in two hours and forty minutes. You need to move.

### Choices
1. Go straight to Commander Voss in ops and force him to listen -> 2
2. Find Dara in engineering—she has been close to cracking the artifact readings -> 3

## Chapter 2
### Title
The Commander's Price

### Content
Voss is running pre-shift ops when you push through the hatch—four subordinates, a wall of screens, and a coffee cup he sets down with the careful deliberateness of someone who has decided before you opened your mouth that you are about to waste his time. He has the kind of face that only moves when it decides to. You have seen that face in a dozen versions of this conversation.

You tell him a Fracture cluster will breach the observation deck at 06:47. He listens without blinking, studies you for three full seconds—not dismissal, appraisal—and tells you to sleep it off.

You have burned loops on Voss before. You know what will move him and what won't. Rank won't. Urgency won't. Pleading certainly won't. But one thing has worked, once.

### Choices
1. Give Voss the exact breach coordinates and timing down to the second -> 4
2. Leave Voss behind and arm the construction workers in ring C yourself -> 5

## Chapter 3
### Title
Dara's Eureka

### Content
You find Dara at her station in engineering, surrounded by empty ration packs and two mugs of coffee she has forgotten to drink. She has been here all night—smudges under her eyes, hair tied back with what looks like a length of cable from the rack—and she is talking quietly to herself as she works through the sensor feed from the sealed vault three decks below. This is different from previous loops. Usually she is bent over her screens. This loop she looks up before you even speak, as if some part of her was already alert to the possibility of company.

She turns the screen toward you without being asked. A waveform pulses in a steady ascending pattern—tight, regular, getting tighter. The artifact, she explains with the barely-contained focus of someone who has been awake too long and found something too important to stop for, is counting down to something. Each oscillation peaks a fraction higher than the last. It is not random decay. It is not passive resonance. It is converging.

She is the first person in any loop who has reached this conclusion on their own. You feel the hairs on your forearms stand up.

### Choices
1. Help Dara isolate the artifact before the Fracture can sync with it -> 6
2. Ask Dara whether the countdown could be redirected—used as a weapon against the Fracture -> 5

## Chapter 4
### Title
Credibility Check

### Content
You tell Voss the coordinates precisely: the strike comes at 06:47:22, outer pane of the observation deck, bearing 224 declination negative twelve. You describe the four-second harmonic resonance the pressure sensors will log before impact—a low bass thrum in the hull plating, like a held note on an instrument no one has built yet—and explain that ops will mistake it for a standard microimpact until it is too late.

Then you wait. You have done this before. The waiting is never easier.

At 06:47:21, the proximity alarm starts screaming. Voss stares at you across the ops table, jaw locked, every fraction of skepticism stripped away in one second. The perimeter array lights up with inbound contacts at the exact bearing, the exact declination. Outside the reinforced viewport, the dark moves.

"Talk fast," he says. His voice is completely level. Whatever Voss is feeling, he has already decided it can wait.

### Choices
1. Lead a coordinated intercept team to the observation deck -> 7
2. Use Voss's authority to seal the artifact vault and force an overload -> 8

## Chapter 5
### Title
The Cascade

### Content
Good intentions collide in the cargo corridors. Armed construction workers pressing toward the observation deck. Dara's engineering team rerouting power to the artifact array without clearance. Station security, alerted by the weapons report, moving to intercept both groups at once. Three factions moving fast and blind, each one certain they are the only rational actors in a crisis no one has briefed any of them on.

The artifact pulses three times in rapid succession—each burst traveling through the deck plates like a physical blow, a concussive wave you feel in your back teeth more than your ears. A frequency you have never recorded in any previous loop. The walls emit a low resonant groan and your vision smears at the edges for one nauseating second. In the engineering bay two sections forward, a pressure coupling blows with a sound like a gunshot.

The Fracture is still eleven minutes out. You are already losing the station from the inside. Every loop you have run on Voss or on Dara alone, you have kept the factions separate. This loop they are in motion together and you are in the middle of the corridor with no clear play left.

### Choices
1. Sprint to the artifact room and contain the pulse before it cascades further -> 6
2. Run for the emergency pods before the structural failure cascade makes escape impossible -> 9

## Chapter 6
### Title
The Artifact Room

### Content
The artifact floats in its magnetic cradle on the lower vault's central platform: a latticed sphere, palm-sized, its surface a mesh of interlocking curves in a material that scatters the vault's utility lights wrong—giving back a faint iridescence that doesn't match any spectral register the station's sensors can name. Up close it hums against your sternum like a second heartbeat, just a half-beat off from your own.

Dara's readings, pulled up on the vault terminal, confirm what you have circled toward across dozens of loops but never been able to prove. The artifact is the loop's anchor point. Every time the Fracture cluster reaches Varda Station, the artifact rewinds time—not as an attack, not as a weapon—but as a fail-safe built by whoever originally placed it here. Its logic is preservation: if the artifact changes hands, reality resets until the conditions of the covenant embedded in its waveform are met.

The Fracture entities aren't invading Varda Station. They are following a distress beacon—this artifact, sending its signal across space for longer than humanity has been in this sector. They are trying to recover something they lost. The seventeen people who have died in every previous loop died not because the Fracture wanted them dead, but because neither the station’s crew nor the Fracture understood what was happening quickly enough to prevent it.

That understanding, standing in this vault with Dara's screen glowing and the artifact humming off-tempo against your ribs, is the first genuinely new thing you have felt in a very long time.

### Choices
1. Sever the artifact's power coupling to collapse the loop without destroying the artifact -> 10
2. Overload the artifact in a controlled detonation—ending the loop and the Fracture threat permanently -> 8

## Chapter 7
### Title
Contact

### Content
Your intercept team reaches the observation deck with seconds to spare before the outer pane cracks. Suits sealed, magnetic anchors set along the deck rail, weapons charged and safeties off—and then you do something no previous loop allowed: you patch into the artifact's active beacon feed from the vault terminal and override its automated distress cycle, replacing it with a structured pulse—three short, three long, three short—shaped from the artifact's own resonance but modulated to show deliberate intent. Not a distress call repeating itself. A conscious reply.

The Fracture entities stop. Through the reinforced glass you see them clearly for the first time, the whole cluster spread across the starfield: dozens of angular silhouettes, dark and jagged against the void, each one distinct, each one held absolutely still in a way that takes your breath away. They are not rock or debris. They are deliberate.

Eleven minutes pass. No shared language. No protocol. No framework any of your training ever built for this. Your team holds its breath and its fire both. On the hull sensors, the cluster's formation slowly shifts—tightening, reorganising—from assault spread to something closer to waiting. Listening.

The artifact pulses once, soft and clear, and goes dark. The loop does not reset.

You stand at the observation deck viewport long after your team lowers their weapons, watching the Fracture cluster hold position in the dark outside the glass. You understand it now: this was never an invasion. The artifact was a distress beacon placed here longer ago than the station's founding, and the Fracture have been answering it across the silence for longer than you can calculate. You were the first person in every loop to understand that. You are the first person in this one to do something with it.

You key your comm. "Hold your fire. All units hold your fire."

The silence that follows is the first genuinely new moment you have experienced in a very long time. You let it last.

## Chapter 8
### Title
Controlled Burn

### Content
The vault's magnetic isolation holds through the pre-detonation sequence, containing the blast cone inside three metres of reinforced alloy. The Fracture cluster collapses inward without its beacon—dropping off every sensor in an instant, like a signal cut at the source. The loop, stripped of its anchor, simply ends. No reset. No copper taste rising at the back of the throat. Just the ordinary, unremarkable now.

Seventeen people who died in every previous loop walk out of the observation deck unaware of how close any of them came. Dara does not walk out.

She was running the containment alignment at the console inside the vault. Magnetic isolation requires constant manual adjustment during the pre-detonation window—the controls must not be released before the sequence completes or the blast cone fails. When the readings destabilise and you reach for the panel, she is already there. She shoves you through the vault hatch with both hands and holds your eyes for exactly one second before the hatch closes. That second contains something you don't have words for. You seal it from outside. The detonation is clean. The vault contains it completely. You are told this, twice, by people who mean it and are at least partly right.

Six weeks later, Varda Station files its incident report under a corporate extraction charter that classifies the artifact, the loop, the Fracture entities—all of it—for thirty years. You give testimony to a closed panel. Dara's name appears in none of the public filings. The people whose lives she helped save will go to sleep tonight not knowing it. The loop is over. You are free of it. The price was real, and you will carry it exactly as long as you live.

## Chapter 9
### Title
The Last Loop

### Content
You sprint hard for ring two, legs burning, the station groaning around you in registers you have never heard. Not the sharp crack of a Fracture breach against the outer pane—something structural, something coming apart at a level below the deck plates. The umbilical locks to the emergency pod bay are already dead when you arrive, sheared by a cascade failure two sections aft.

You stand at the sealed pod hatch with four seconds left and notice there is no copper taste at the back of your throat.

Every loop you have lived through—every reset, every death, fast or slow or in a suit that wouldn't seal—the copper taste arrived first. An early signal, something in the boundary between this moment and the next, pulling at the edges of your consciousness like a tide drawing back before it turns. It is not here. The artifact, burning somewhere below you in a cascade the station can no longer contain, has spent its last pulse. The loop's anchor is gone.

This is not a reset. There is no next loop. There is only the station's last sound, low and final, and the dark outside the sheared viewport.

Varda Station is gone.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 10
### Title
Severance

### Content
Dara cuts the power coupling in one precise movement while you brace the magnetic cradle with both hands against the artifact's resistance. The hum against your sternum—your constant companion through every version of this day—drops in pitch, stutters twice, and goes completely still. The resonance signature on her screen flattens to a line.

Outside, the Fracture cluster completes its final approach and then disperses. Without the beacon, there is nothing to home on. One by one, the angular shapes separate into the dark with the unhurried drift of things that have somewhere else to be and are no longer in a hurry.

Varda Station runs its damage assessments on a breach that never came. Voss files a proximity alert and closes it within the hour. The seventeen people who have died in every previous version of this morning eat breakfast and argue about the duty roster and notice nothing unusual about the day.

Dara looks at you, confused and a little concerned. "You okay? You look like you've been awake for a week."

"Longer than that," you say.

You sit down on the floor of the artifact room—the magnetic cradle humming with ordinary, empty electricity above you—and you start writing. Every loop. Every death, yours and other people's. Every small adjustment you made and whether it helped. Every face you learned to recognise, couldn't save, and then saved, and couldn't tell. You write until your hand cramps and then keep writing. You sign your name at the bottom. You feel, for the first time in a span your memory cannot fully measure, like a person with a future instead of a loop.

The debrief takes five weeks. The station council votes unanimously to publish the full artifact record. Whatever the Fracture were answering when they crossed all that dark and silence, the record should show that someone here finally understood. Neither species can yet translate the other. But the beacon called, and something came, and you are no longer standing in the same second over and over—and for now, that is more than enough.

### Choices
The End
