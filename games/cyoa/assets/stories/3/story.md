# The Last Broadcast

## Keywords
- space
- signal
- survival

## Chapter 1
### Title
Silence Over Earth

### Content
You are on comms watch in Meridian Station's cupola when Earth goes silent at 03:12 UTC.

Not static. Not interference. Silence.

Ground control's heartbeat beacon dies first. Commercial bands follow in a clean cascade. Even the encrypted military channels that never stop, not for weather, not for war, not for politics, drop in the same second. Through the cupola glass, city grids dim in coordinated waves moving east across North America, as if darkness has been instructed where to go. Ozone from an overworked console fan catches in the back of your throat.

Okafor, your station partner, drags a new trace onto the spectral analyzer and freezes. One transmission now occupies a Cold War emergency frequency that should be dead: precise cadence, non-human timing, and a repeating coordinate in northern Canada. The signal does not request contact. It transmits as if it already accounted for you.

You and Okafor trade one look over the console. No one briefs you for this moment. If you answer, you authorize first contact protocol without a chain of command. If you do not, you commit to descending blind toward a world that just changed shape.

### Choices
1. Reply to the signal with a structured contact protocol -> 2
2. Ignore the signal and begin immediate re-entry planning -> 3

## Chapter 2
### Title
Prime Number Voice

### Content
You key the channel and transmit prime numbers: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11.

The return sequence mirrors yours without delay, continues past your stop point, and then shifts into plain English text. The language is grammatically clean and emotionally wrong, like a perfect translation from a source that does not contain hesitation: DO NOT LAND YET. ATMOSPHERIC ADAPTATION EVENT IN PROGRESS. ORBITAL DATA REQUIRED AT CONVERGENCE COUNCIL.

Okafor mutters, "It sounds like a committee memo from the end of the world." He does not take his eyes off the checksum pane.

More text follows: survivor groups are converging at the transmitted coordinates; a crisis council is forming; your orbital sensors can verify spread patterns no ground team can observe in full. The sender identifies itself as contingency infrastructure and explicitly invites verification, not trust.

You can commit now and descend before the political window closes, or spend precious time validating what the message claims. One choice prioritizes speed. The other prioritizes certainty. Neither option lets you keep both.

### Choices
1. Stay in orbit briefly and collect independent evidence first -> 4
2. Treat the signal as high-risk and begin descent now -> 3

## Chapter 3
### Title
Forced Descent

### Content
You deorbit without a filed plan because there is no one left who can clear one.

Re-entry is rougher than your model projected. Weather cells have reorganized into corridors the simulator cannot classify. During plasma blackout, fragmented ground traffic slips through your audio stack anyway: convoy frequencies, triage requests, one voice repeating the same name in French until the carrier drops.

You exit blackout into amber sky with particulate density outside current hazard tables. Through the descent window, the surface below shows crystalline bloom across former soil bands, reflecting sunlight in spectral colors your instrumentation labels as unknown material interaction.

Two signals survive the noise. One: moving convoy transponders north of your projected touchdown, tight formation, consistent spacing, disciplined routing. Two: a high-power council broadcast at the same coordinates as the original transmission, issuing assembly protocols and requesting technical witnesses. Your descent monitor shows eighteen percent maneuvering fuel remaining, enough for one meaningful trajectory correction and not enough for regret.

### Choices
1. Divert to the moving survivor convoy -> 5
2. Land near the council site to enter the vote immediately -> 6

## Chapter 4
### Title
Evidence Window

### Content
You spend six continuous hours pulling multispectral sweeps across every orbital track you can hold without burning too much station maneuvering fuel.

You map isotope plumes from coordinates with no matching industrial infrastructure. You map migration clusters moving in four synchronized streams toward the Canadian interior. You map the atmospheric adaptation front and discover it is not random spread; it advances in pulses tied to tower emissions and wind shear channels. The planet is still organizing. Something is governing the transition.

You package your dataset with confidence intervals, error bounds, and geotemporal overlays. The work steadies your hands but not your breathing. If you descend with this archive, the council cannot argue from rumor. If you remain above, you can continue documenting changes no one on the ground can see, but the vote below is scheduled to close in nine hours.

### Choices
1. Descend with the full dataset and testify in person -> 6
2. Stay in orbit and prioritize long-range archival continuity -> 9

## Chapter 5
### Title
Ground Convoy

### Content
You touch down hard on terrain that should be soil and sounds like broken glass under your boots.

The convoy reaches you before you finish post-landing checks: forty-three survivors in three vehicles, running disciplined rotation on watch, fuel, and medical triage. Their lead, Ilyas, briefs while walking, not stopping. "We don't need another speech," he says. "We need someone the council can't accuse of guessing."

He outlines the deadlock. The Acceleration Directorate's goal is immediate species-level survival through mandatory transformation and centralized tower control: faster adaptation, lower immediate mortality, no refusal period. The Continuity Compact's goal is survival with retained civil legitimacy through consent-based protocols: voluntary transformation, protected baseline enclaves, and independent oversight even if the casualty curve rises short-term. Both factions control parts of the tower network. Both claim science. Both accuse the other of sentencing millions.

On the ridgeline ahead, adaptation towers pulse in sequence through ion haze like a metronome for decisions already late. Ilyas can take you to the chamber where your orbital perspective may force a viable compromise. Or you can try to disable tower capacity directly and bet that interruption buys humanity time.

### Choices
1. Go with Ilyas to the Convergence Council -> 6
2. Attempt direct tower sabotage before the vote locks -> 8

## Chapter 6
### Title
The Convergence Vote

### Content
The chamber is a repurposed research station forty meters underground, over capacity, under-ventilated, and running on generator heat that makes every argument shorter and sharper.

Directorate envoys sit on the east tier under a projected mortality board. Their lead scientist, Dr. Sera Vonn, keeps one hand on the model stream while she speaks in clipped projections: "Delay is not neutral. Delay selects who dies first." Compact delegates occupy the west tier under legal protocol displays. Their lead counsel, Mara El-Tayeb, answers with the same controlled calm each time: "Compulsion is not neutral either. Once consent is suspended, it does not return by apology."

You testify to the sequence from orbit: synchronized blackout, pulsed atmospheric spread, tower-linked acceleration windows, and the survivability envelope your data identifies if adaptation remains voluntary but phased. The room goes quiet in a way that means no one got what they wanted and everyone got something they have to account for.

When the chair calls for recommendation, you understand the real choice. You can prioritize immediate casualty minimization with mandatory acceleration, or defend a consent framework that preserves agency at measurable cost.

### Choices
1. Endorse mandatory acceleration under emergency authority -> 7
2. Defend phased consent with transparent oversight -> 10

## Chapter 7
### Title
Survival Without Choice

### Content
You endorse the Directorate plan.

The rollout is brutally efficient. Tower schedules tighten. Adaptation access expands by mandate. Mortality drops faster than conservative models predicted, then stabilizes below worst-case projections. Entire regions that would have collapsed remain biologically viable because the timeline was forced ahead of fear.

Resistance enclaves are reclassified as noncompliant risk zones. "Stabilization" becomes administrative vocabulary for interventions no one can reverse. The Directorate insists these measures are temporary. Temporary lasts long enough to become architecture.

Years later, records keep your name in two columns. In one, you are the witness whose testimony prevented civilizational die-off. In the other, you are the witness who normalized survival by compulsion. Both records are accurate. They are not competing verdicts; they are the same verdict, joined at the point where your recommendation became policy and policy became people's bodies.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 8
### Title
The Last Burn

### Content
You choose towers over chamber speeches and move alone through ion fog with tools meant for maintenance, not demolition.

The first node fails partially after six minutes of manual bypass. Output drops, then rebounds through an automatic reroute you did not know existed. The second node sits inside a containment trench with a ventilation system tuned for stable operation, not forced breach. When your improvised cut opens the casing, ionized particulate blasts upward and your suit filters start losing capacity in under thirty seconds.

You stop pretending this becomes a return trip.

With failing lungs and one functioning uplink, you transmit everything in packet bursts: node topography, hazard signatures, projected spread maps, convoy routes, and the evidence archive you carried from orbit. Every public and emergency frequency. No encryption. No gatekeeping. You hear your own breathing go ragged in your headset as the final packet leaves.

Whether anyone receives it in time is not a question you survive to answer.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 9
### Title
Orbit of One

### Content
You remain above and keep recording.

At first the decision feels disciplined: preserve a complete account while ground actors make policy with incomplete visibility. You map adaptation pulses, tower outages, enclave migration, and the narrowing of habitable bands with a rigor no council memo can match. The archive grows into the most comprehensive external witness record of the transition.

Then resupply windows close.

You reallocate power from nonessential modules. You cut meals by percentage, then by day. You keep the transmitter alive as long as station physics allows, feeding downlinked packets to any receiver still functioning. At each cycle, you still run checksum verification on outbound archives, the same ritual Okafor watched in silence when the first message arrived. Below, the world keeps changing in ways your instruments can describe and your hands cannot influence.

By the time you can no longer stand through a full watch cycle, the archive has become your only remaining task. Meridian drifts on corrected inertia and failing reserves. You do not reach the surface again. Your dataset does. Later generations reconstruct critical decisions from the trail you left them, and history calls that contribution indispensable. It is also lonely in a way language cannot make useful.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 10
### Title
The Human Clause

### Content
You defend phased consent and insist the vote bind every protocol to external audit.

The final framework, ratified after fourteen more hours of closed-floor negotiation, becomes known as the Human Clause: no involuntary modification, protected baseline enclaves, rotating independent oversight, and phased adaptation windows tied to the survivability envelope your orbital data proved viable. It is neither side's ideal victory. That is why it passes.

The cost is real. Casualties remain higher than under full mandate during the first phase. You attend briefings where each number carries a name someone else still says in present tense. But the line holds: people choose, and institutions must persuade rather than compel.

A year later, tower policy hearings are public. Directorate scientists and Compact advocates still fight in language both sides now understand cannot bypass consent. Dr. Sera Vonn signs an audit charter she once argued would cost too many lives. Mara El-Tayeb prosecutes a coercion case under the Clause and wins on appeal. Okafor leaves orbital operations for treaty verification and sends field notes with excellent math. The species that emerges is altered, tired, and still capable of argument without automatic force. You do not call it triumph. You call it survival with authorship intact, which is close enough to hope that you can finally sleep through a full station-night.

### Choices
The End
