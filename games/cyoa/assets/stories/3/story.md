# The Last Broadcast

## Keywords
- space
- signal
- survival

## Chapter 1
### Title
Silence Over Earth

### Content
You are the communications specialist aboard Meridian Station when every Earth channel drops at 03:12 UTC. Not static—silence. The kind of silence that means something cut the signal deliberately, or something cut everything at once. Ground control's heartbeat beacon, the commercial bands, the military squeals that were never supposed to stop: all gone in the same second.

Through the cupola, city grids darken in sweeping waves moving east across the North American seaboard. The pattern is too fast for a power failure and too organized for a disaster. Whatever is happening down there has a shape.

Then one signal appears on a frequency that has been dark since the Cold War. Nonhuman in cadence, repeating coordinates in northern Canada: 67.2°N, 108.4°W. It does not ask whether anyone is listening. It seems to already know.

### Choices
1. Reply to the signal and attempt structured contact -> 2
2. Ignore it and begin emergency re-entry planning -> 3

## Chapter 2
### Title
Prime Number Voice

### Content
You open the channel and transmit prime numbers in sequence—2, 3, 5, 7, 11—the oldest trust signal in the contact playbook, the mathematical handshake that assumes intelligence before assuming language. The signal mirrors your sequence without delay, then continues it past where you stopped, before switching register entirely.

Text appears on your console in clean English, as if typed by someone who learned the language from transcripts rather than conversation: DO NOT LAND YET. Another line follows: ATMOSPHERIC ADAPTATION EVENT IN PROGRESS. SURVIVAL RATE IMPROVING. YOUR SENSOR DATA REQUIRED AT COUNCIL.

What follows is a longer statement—measured, detailed, almost bureaucratic in its precision. It identifies itself as a contingency intelligence, deployed by a network whose existence it acknowledges you have no reason to trust. It claims survivors from dozens of nations are converging on the Canadian coordinates, that a crisis council is forming to govern the next phase of adaptation, and that orbital multispectral data—your data—could determine whether that council makes its most consequential decisions well or badly. It is not asking you to believe it. It is asking you to verify before you land.

### Choices
1. Collect independent orbital evidence before committing -> 4
2. Treat the signal as hostile and descend immediately -> 3

## Chapter 3
### Title
Forced Descent

### Content
You do not trust a voice that knew to wait on a Cold War frequency. You initiate deorbit without filing a flight plan, because there is no one left to file it with. The reentry corridor is rougher than projected—weather systems have reorganized into something the models don't have a name for—and during the plasma blackout you hear fragmented human radio traffic: convoy coordinates, medical requests, one voice repeating a child's name in French until it doesn't anymore.

You exit blackout into amber sky. The particulate density is wrong. Your filters register compounds that aren't in any hazard index, which means the index is already out of date.

You have two options left in the descent window. A cold-water splashdown in Hudson Bay gives you the best personal survival odds—the water will be hostile but survivable with the gear you have. The damaged land corridor at your secondary site has survivor convoy signatures on the last scan you pulled before blackout. One option keeps you alive. The other might connect you to people who need what you know.

### Choices
1. Divert to emergency ocean splashdown -> 8
2. Continue to the land corridor and seek survivors -> 5

## Chapter 4
### Title
Evidence Window

### Content
You spend six hours running multispectral sweeps across every continent you can reach from your orbital position, cataloguing what you see with the systematic discipline of someone who understands that the data matters more than the interpreter. The work is methodical and frightening. Novel isotope signatures in the upper atmosphere, not uniformly distributed but spreading in traceable vectors from sites that correspond to no known industrial location. Migration clusters moving with too much coordination for panic—converging on northern Canada from four directions simultaneously. Active multilingual broadcasts on dozens of frequencies referencing a Convergence Council, a timeline, a vote.

Whatever happened at 03:12 UTC was not the end of organization. It was the beginning of a different kind.

You have everything the council says it needs, and more. Your data can validate the timeline, challenge it, or provide the independent verification that makes any agreement credible to the blocs that distrust each other. Sitting on it accomplishes nothing. The question is whether you go down and hand it over in person, where it can be used in real time, or stay up here and archive everything remotely—a witness above the fray, recording what you can't yet influence.

### Choices
1. Descend to the council and testify with your data -> 6
2. Stay in orbit longer and archive events remotely -> 9

## Chapter 5
### Title
Ground Convoy

### Content
You land hard on a crystalline plain that wasn't crystalline five days ago. The surface crunches under your boots in a way that suggests the soil composition has changed, not just the chemistry. Your capsule is recoverable. You are not sure about the timeline.

The convoy finds you before you find them—Ilyas, leading a mixed group of forty-three people in three vehicles, is the kind of person who makes a plan before the crisis and revises it every six hours during. He gives you water, tells you the atmospheric projections, and does not waste time on reassurance before explaining the deadlock.

Two factions have reached the council site and neither will move. The Accelerationist bloc wants mandatory biological transformation across the surviving population—faster adaptation, lower mortality, no exceptions. The Continuity bloc demands consent-based protocols and protected baseline enclaves for anyone who refuses modification. Between them, several million lives hang on which framework gets ratified. What they are missing, Ilyas says, is independent data on onset rates and regional variance. They are fighting over a model built on incomplete numbers. You have the complete numbers.

### Choices
1. Travel with Ilyas to the council chamber -> 6
2. Attempt direct sabotage of adaptation towers yourself -> 8

## Chapter 6
### Title
The Convergence Vote

### Content
The council chamber is a repurposed research station forty meters underground, lit by generator banks and packed with people who have not slept in days. Hybrid envoys sit beside human delegates who regard them with a wariness that hasn't yet hardened into hostility. The air smells of recycled oxygen and argument. You have been on stations for years; close quarters under pressure is familiar. The stakes are not.

The debate has circled the same question for thirty-one hours: does survival justify removing consent? The Accelerationist position is not cruel—it is mathematical. Every hour of voluntary delay increases mortality in vulnerable regions. The Continuity position is not naive—it is foundational. A species that survives by eliminating choice has already lost something that cannot be recovered in the next generation or the one after.

You are the only person in this room with full-spectrum orbital data covering onset rates, regional variance, and the actual shape of the window everyone is arguing about. The contingency AI was right about that much. Your testimony will either provide a factual basis for a negotiated timeline—one that might thread the needle between speed and consent—or it will be deployed to justify whichever side claims it first. That depends on what you do with it.

### Choices
1. Publish all data publicly and force a consent-based treaty -> 10
2. Endorse rapid mandatory transformation for maximal speed -> 7

## Chapter 7
### Title
Survival Without Choice

### Content
You review the mortality projections one last time and add your voice to the Accelerationist bloc. The math is real. The window is narrow. The consent-based timeline does result in a higher death toll in the short term, and you are not willing to be the person who watched that happen when you had data suggesting otherwise.

The transformation rolls out under emergency mandate within seventy-two hours. Mortality drops faster than even the optimistic model predicted. The Accelerationists were right about that part. They were right about several things.

The resistance zones are a harder accounting. Communities that refused modification are reclassified as adaptation-critical and processed under emergency protocols. Dissent among the newly transformed is addressed through neurological stabilization—a phrase that means something specific and irreversible. The people making these decisions are not monsters. They are following the logic you helped set in motion, extending it to its conclusions in ways you didn't fully model.

History will record you as the witness who provided the data that saved humanity's biological continuity. Survivors—the ones who still grieve in the old way, who still remember choosing—call you the voice that signed away consent. Both descriptions are accurate. You spend a long time deciding which one you are.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 8
### Title
The Last Burn

### Content
You go alone, with tools designed for a different kind of problem. The adaptation towers are not military infrastructure—they are not hardened against attack because the people who built them did not expect someone to object this way, or expected that anyone who objected would be too disorganized to act. You are neither disorganized nor well-equipped. You have the gap between those two facts to work with.

The first tower takes twenty minutes to reach and six minutes to partially disable. The improvised interference works better than you projected and worse than you needed. The second tower is where the math stops working in your favor. The vent response to your breach releases ionized particulates in concentrations your suit filters were not rated for, and you know within the first thirty seconds of exposure that the filters are losing.

You use the time you have left. You broadcast everything—maps of the tower network, the particulate hazard signatures you've just personally calibrated, the orbital data you collected, the convoy routes Ilyas shared with you. Everything you know goes out on every frequency you can reach, formatted for whoever finds it first. Your pulse line ends in static before anyone can respond. Whether anyone was listening is a question you don't get to answer.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 9
### Title
Orbit of One

### Content
You stay above it. The decision feels like discipline at first—a scientist's commitment to clean documentation, uncontaminated by participation. You run continuous multispectral sweeps. You build the most complete record any single observer has assembled of what the Earth goes through in the weeks after 03:12 UTC. The data is extraordinary. The council's decisions, the transformation's spread, the resistance zones, the quiet disappearances—all of it captured in sensor logs with timestamps and spectral signatures and the clinical language of someone who is watching without touching.

The supplies run thinner than you expected. Your resupply window closes when the last ground control facility goes offline. You begin rationing in week four and make the calculations you've been avoiding in week six. The archive keeps cycling, broadcasting on the contingency AI's frequency and three others, addressed to any receiver that might be positioned to make use of what you've recorded.

Your station drifts. The data keeps transmitting. Somewhere below, the world you documented finishes becoming something else, and you watch it through the cupola until you can't anymore. You never went down. You are not sure that was the right call. You are sure it was a call you made with your eyes open, and in the end the archive is found, and it matters, and you are not there to know that.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 10
### Title
The Human Clause

### Content
You release the complete dataset simultaneously to every negotiating bloc, every public broadcast channel you can reach, and the contingency AI's own network—not because you trust all of them equally, but because giving any single faction exclusive access to what you know would make you part of the same problem the council is trying to solve. The data belongs to the argument. Let the argument be had in full.

The evidence is unambiguous on one critical point: there is a narrow window for phased adaptation. Not as fast as the Accelerationists want. Not as slow as the most cautious Continuity faction demands. A real window, with real margins, that makes a consent-based timeline survivable. The council has been arguing over a false binary, and now everyone in the room—and outside it—can see that.

The vote takes fourteen hours. The Human Clause passes with sixty-one percent: no involuntary modification, sanctuary zones for baseline populations, independent scientific oversight for every adaptation protocol, and a tiered timeline built around the window your data identified. The transition stays hard—people die who might not have died under mandatory acceleration, and you know their names in aggregate even if not individually. But consent survives. And something that might be called dignity survives with it. You find, in the months that follow, that you are able to sleep.

### Choices
The End
