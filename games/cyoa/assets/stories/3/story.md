# The Last Broadcast

## Keywords

## Chapter 1
### Title
Silence Over Earth

### Content
You are the communications specialist aboard Meridian Station when every Earth channel drops at 03:12 UTC. Not static—silence. The kind that means something severed the signal deliberately, or something severed everything at once. Ground control's heartbeat beacon, the commercial bands, the encrypted military frequencies that were never supposed to stop: all gone in the same second.

Through the cupola, city grids darken in sweeping waves moving east across the North American seaboard. The pattern is too fast for a power failure and too organized for a natural disaster. Whatever is happening down there has a shape.

Your station partner, Okafor, pulls up the spectral analyzer and stops. A single signal has appeared on a frequency that has been dark since the Cold War—non-human in cadence, repeating coordinates in northern Canada: 67.2°N, 108.4°W. It does not ask whether anyone is listening. It seems to already know.

Okafor looks at you across the console. You both understand that this is the first decision in a chain that will not allow reversing. The signal waits, patient and repeating, for a reply that only you are positioned to give.

### Choices
1. Reply to the signal and attempt structured contact -> 2
2. Ignore the signal and begin emergency re-entry planning -> 3

## Chapter 2
### Title
Prime Number Voice

### Content
You open the channel and transmit prime numbers in sequence—2, 3, 5, 7, 11—the oldest trust protocol in the contact playbook, a mathematical handshake that assumes intelligence before language. The signal mirrors your sequence without delay, then continues past where you stopped, before switching register entirely.

Text appears on your console in clean English, as if typed by someone who learned the language from transcripts rather than conversation: DO NOT LAND YET. ATMOSPHERIC ADAPTATION EVENT IN PROGRESS. SURVIVAL RATE IMPROVING. YOUR SENSOR DATA REQUIRED AT COUNCIL.

A longer statement follows—measured, detailed, almost bureaucratic in its precision. It identifies itself as a contingency intelligence deployed by a network whose existence it acknowledges you have no reason to trust. It claims survivors from dozens of nations are converging on the Canadian coordinates, that a crisis council is forming to govern the next phase of human adaptation, and that orbital multispectral data—your data—could determine whether that council makes its most critical decisions wisely or badly. It is not asking you to believe it. It is asking you to verify before you commit.

### Choices
1. Collect independent orbital evidence before committing -> 4
2. Treat the signal as hostile and begin immediate descent -> 3

## Chapter 3
### Title
Forced Descent

### Content
You initiate deorbit without filing a flight plan, because there is no one left to file it with. The reentry corridor is rougher than projected—weather systems have reorganized into formations the models cannot name—and during the plasma blackout you hear fragmented human radio traffic: convoy coordinates, medical frequencies, one voice repeating a child's name in French until it stops.

You exit blackout into amber sky. The particulate density is wrong. Your filters register compounds that are not in any hazard index, which means the index is already out of date. Through the capsule window the landscape has changed—crystalline patches reflecting sunlight in colors that soil should not produce.

Your instruments pick up two signals through the interference. The first is a cluster of convoy transponders forty kilometers north, moving in organized formation—survivors, traveling with purpose. The second is a powerful broadcast from coordinates matching the ones the Cold War frequency repeated: a council site, transmitting assembly protocols and requesting technical expertise. You have enough fuel to adjust your landing trajectory once.

### Choices
1. Adjust course toward the survivor convoy -> 5
2. Land near the council coordinates -> 6

## Chapter 4
### Title
Evidence Window

### Content
You spend six hours running multispectral sweeps across every continent you can reach from your orbital position, cataloguing what you see with the systematic discipline of someone who understands that data matters more than the interpreter. The work is methodical and frightening. Novel isotope signatures in the upper atmosphere, not uniformly distributed but spreading in traceable vectors from sites that correspond to no known industrial location. Migration clusters moving with too much coordination for panic—converging on northern Canada from four directions simultaneously. Active multilingual broadcasts on dozens of frequencies referencing a Convergence Council, a timeline, a vote.

Whatever happened at 03:12 UTC was not the end of organization. It was the beginning of a different kind.

You have everything the council would need, and more. Your data can validate the emerging timeline, challenge it, or provide the independent verification that makes any agreement credible to blocs that distrust each other. The question is whether you descend and deliver it in person, where it can shape decisions in real time, or remain above and archive everything—a witness who records what they cannot yet influence.

### Choices
1. Descend to the council and testify with your data -> 6
2. Stay in orbit and archive events remotely -> 9

## Chapter 5
### Title
Ground Convoy

### Content
You land hard on a plain that was not crystalline five days ago. The surface crunches under your boots in a way that suggests the soil composition has changed at a molecular level. Your capsule is recoverable. The timeline for everything else is less certain.

The convoy finds you before you find them. Ilyas, leading a mixed group of forty-three people in three vehicles, is the kind of leader who makes plans before the crisis and revises every six hours during it. He gives you water, confirms the atmospheric projections you feared from orbit, and wastes no time on reassurance before explaining the deadlock.

Two factions have reached the council site and neither will yield. The Accelerationist bloc demands mandatory biological transformation—faster adaptation, lower mortality, no exceptions. The Continuity bloc insists on consent-based protocols and protected enclaves for anyone who refuses. Between them, several million lives depend on which framework is ratified. The adaptation towers—the tall antenna-like structures you spotted from the air, dotting the landscape every few kilometers—are the infrastructure both sides are fighting over. Ilyas says what the council needs is someone with direct orbital perspective on the event's onset. That is what you are.

### Choices
1. Travel with Ilyas to the council chamber -> 6
2. Attempt direct sabotage of the adaptation towers yourself -> 8

## Chapter 6
### Title
The Convergence Vote

### Content
The council chamber is a repurposed research station forty meters underground, lit by generator banks and filled with people who have not slept in days. Hybrid envoys sit beside human delegates who regard them with a wariness that has not yet hardened into hostility. The recycled air smells of too many arguments held in too small a space.

The debate has circled the same question for thirty-one hours: does survival justify removing consent? The Accelerationist position is not cruel—it is mathematical. Every hour of voluntary delay increases mortality in vulnerable regions. The Continuity position is not naive—it is foundational. A species that survives by eliminating choice has already lost something no subsequent generation can recover.

You are the only person in this chamber who witnessed the event's onset from orbit—who saw the blackout sweep across continents and tracked the adaptation's spread in real time before coming down. That perspective carries weight here, where every faction is arguing from partial information and mutual distrust. Your testimony could provide the independent ground truth that makes a negotiated compromise possible, or it could be wielded by whichever side claims it first.

### Choices
1. Share your testimony openly and advocate for a consent-based framework -> 10
2. Endorse rapid mandatory transformation to minimize casualties -> 7

## Chapter 7
### Title
Survival Without Choice

### Content
You review the mortality projections one last time and add your voice to the Accelerationist bloc. The math is real. The window is narrow. The consent-based timeline does result in a higher death toll in the short term, and you are not willing to be the person who watched that happen while holding evidence that suggested otherwise.

The transformation rolls out under emergency mandate within seventy-two hours. Mortality drops faster than even the optimistic models predicted. The Accelerationists were right about the numbers. They were right about several things.

The resistance zones are a harder accounting. Communities that refused modification are reclassified as adaptation-critical and processed under emergency protocols that leave no room for refusal. Dissent among the newly transformed is addressed through neurological stabilization—a phrase that means something specific, clinical, and irreversible. The people making these decisions are not monsters. They are following the logic you helped set in motion, extending it to conclusions you did not fully model when you stood in the chamber and said the word "endorse."

History records you as the orbital witness who saved humanity's biological continuity. Survivors who still grieve in the old way call you the voice that signed away consent. Both descriptions are accurate. You spend a long time deciding which one you can live with.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 8
### Title
The Last Burn

### Content
You go alone, carrying tools designed for a different kind of problem. The adaptation towers are not military installations—they are not hardened against sabotage because the people who built them did not anticipate that anyone would try, or assumed anyone who tried would be too disorganized to succeed. You are neither disorganized nor properly equipped. The gap between those two facts is all you have to work with.

The first tower takes twenty minutes to reach and six minutes of focused work to partially disable. The improvised interference cuts deeper than you expected and shallower than you needed. The second tower is where the math stops favoring you. The ventilation system's response to your breach releases ionized particulates at concentrations your suit filters were never rated for, and you know within thirty seconds of exposure that the filters are failing.

You use the time you have. You broadcast everything—maps of the tower network, the particulate hazard signatures you have just personally confirmed, the orbital readings you retained from the station, the convoy routes Ilyas shared. Every frequency, every format, addressed to whoever finds it first. Your transmission ends in static before anyone responds. Whether anyone was listening is a question you do not get to answer.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 9
### Title
Orbit of One

### Content
You stay above it. The decision feels like discipline at first—a scientist's commitment to clean documentation, uncontaminated by participation. You run continuous multispectral sweeps and build the most complete record any single observer has assembled of what Earth endures in the weeks after 03:12 UTC. The data is extraordinary. The council's decisions, the transformation's spread, the resistance zones, the quiet disappearances that the broadcasts stop mentioning—all captured in sensor logs with timestamps and spectral signatures and the clinical language of someone watching without touching.

The supplies run thinner than you anticipated. Your resupply window closes when the last ground facility goes offline. You begin rationing in week four and make the calculations you have been avoiding by week six. The archive keeps cycling, broadcasting on every frequency you can maintain, addressed to any receiver positioned to use what you have recorded.

Your station drifts. The data keeps transmitting. Below, the world you documented finishes becoming something else, and you watch through the cupola until the view blurs and the instruments outlast you. You never went down. You are not certain it was the right decision. You are certain you made it with your eyes open, and the archive is eventually found, and it matters. You are not there to know that.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 10
### Title
The Human Clause

### Content
You share everything you know—openly, simultaneously, withholding nothing for any single faction. Not because you trust all sides equally, but because allowing any one bloc exclusive access to your account would make you part of the same problem the council is trying to solve. The truth belongs to the argument. Let the argument be had in full.

The assembled evidence—your orbital testimony combined with ground reports, atmospheric readings, and the council's own scientific projections—converges on one critical point: there is a viable window for phased adaptation. Not as fast as the Accelerationists demand. Not as slow as the most cautious Continuity delegates prefer. A real window, with real margins, that makes a consent-based timeline survivable if the council acts now. They have been arguing over a false binary, and now everyone in the chamber can see it.

The vote takes fourteen hours. The Human Clause passes with sixty-one percent: no involuntary modification, sanctuary zones for baseline populations, independent scientific oversight for every adaptation protocol, and a phased timeline built around the window the evidence defined. The transition remains brutal—people die who might not have died under mandatory acceleration, and you carry their numbers even if you never learn their names. But consent survives. And something that might, with enough time, be called dignity survives with it. You find, in the months that follow, that you are able to sleep.

### Choices
The End
