## Chapter 1
### Title
The Open Sarcophagus

### Content
The royal sarcophagus is open from the inside. You stand in the Egyptian wing at 12:07 AM, flashlight beam trembling across a lid that weighs four hundred kilograms and should require a hydraulic lift to move. Four parallel grooves score the interior stone—claw marks, deliberate, almost alphabetic, gouged by something with the patience to carve a message no one was meant to read.

You are the head of night security at Hollow Moon Museum. Two months ago, a theft was pinned on you—circumstantial evidence, an unsigned incident report, and a suspension that ended your credibility before you could defend it. Tonight is your first shift back. If you fail again, whoever framed you last time won't need to try a third.

Motion sensors are firing in three wings simultaneously: the Egyptian gallery, the maritime corridor, and the restricted archive basement. Three alerts at once means coordination—someone who knows the patrol route, the sensor grid, and which alarm you'll answer first. This is not a break-in. It is a performance.

Your radio crackles. Dispatch confirms the alerts but has no camera feeds—maintenance killed the closed-circuit system at 11 PM for a firmware update that should have taken twenty minutes. You are functionally blind. Detective Sarah Chen covers overnight incidents at the museum and can trigger a full lockdown within twelve minutes. But calling her means trusting the same institutional machinery that buried you two months ago without a second look.

### Choices
1. Sweep the galleries alone and track the intruders on your terms -> 2
2. Call Detective Chen and trigger an immediate lockdown -> 3

## Chapter 2
### Title
Split Trail

### Content
You kill your flashlight and follow the sound. Muddy boot prints track through the staff corridor—two sets, different treads, moving fast—and split at the freight elevator. One trail descends toward the archive basement, where off-ledger storage holds items the public collection has never acknowledged. The other leads toward the administrative wing and Director Hartwell's corner office.

Through the cracked office door, two voices argue in low, urgent tones. Someone says "the moon tablet" and someone else says "phase two before dawn." You flatten against the wall. Moon tablet—the name appears on exactly one document you've seen, a restricted acquisition log flagged during your wrongful-termination review. Whoever is inside that office tonight has access, motive, and a deadline.

The basement trail is going cold. Mud dries fast on polished concrete, and within minutes you won't be able to track direction or count how many people went down. But the voices in Hartwell's office are giving you what the basement can't: names, intent, and a direct connection to whoever staged tonight's distraction.

Every instinct from twelve years of security work says secure the physical scene first—evidence doesn't lie, and it doesn't leave when it hears you coming. But evidence also doesn't explain itself. The people behind that door might.

### Choices
1. Follow the basement trail before it goes cold and secure physical evidence -> 4
2. Stay at Hartwell's door and identify the voices first -> 5

## Chapter 3
### Title
Chen on Scene

### Content
Detective Sarah Chen arrives four minutes after your call—fast enough that she must have been nearby before you dialed, or already moving toward the museum on her own schedule. She wears her sidearm and carries a wire kit in her jacket, as though she dressed for this particular night.

She identifies the crescent sigil painted on the sarcophagus lid without hesitation: the Midnight Collective, a trafficking ring that moves anomalous artifacts through private auction networks spanning three continents. Hollow Moon Museum, she explains, has been on a federal watchlist for six months. Director Hartwell's name appears in sealed transport manifests—a convoy is scheduled to leave the building before dawn.

Chen asks you to wear a wire and help her identify an internal accomplice. She speaks with the clipped authority of someone briefing a subordinate, not consulting a witness. Her knowledge is detailed and immediate. It also feels rehearsed—her account maps tonight's events as if she drafted the timeline before hearing yours.

The crescent sigil is still wet on the sarcophagus stone. You press a fingertip to the edge: thick lacquer, recently applied, the kind of theatrical dressing meant to be found on schedule. Someone wanted this discovery to happen at a specific time. Whether Chen is ahead of that schedule or running on it herself, you cannot yet tell.

### Choices
1. Cooperate fully with Chen—accept the wire and share everything you know -> 6
2. Keep her informed but verify everything she tells you independently -> 5

## Chapter 4
### Title
The Hidden Vault

### Content
The basement trail ends at a false shelving unit that swings on concealed hinges when you push the right edge. Behind it: an unlisted vault, climate-controlled, lit by blue LEDs that hum at a frequency you feel in your teeth. Off-ledger artifacts sit in foam-cut cases—eleven occupied, one empty. The empty slot is labelled MOON TABLET — LN-0047, and the security timestamp on the removal reads 11:58 PM. Nine minutes before the sarcophagus alarm.

You photograph everything. The transfer log carries Director Hartwell's credentials—her digital signature, her PIN timestamp, her access clearance level. Either she moved the tablet herself or someone with her exact authorization did, and both roads lead to the same office three floors above.

A second document sits beneath the log: a shipping manifest routed to a private dock on the river, departure window 04:00–04:30 AM. The convoy is real. The timeline matches the voices you overheard upstairs. You hold physical proof that the museum's own director authorized removal of a restricted artifact hours before an elaborate incursion made it look like someone else's crime.

The question is who receives this next. Confronting Hartwell directly means walking into her office with leverage—but also into whatever protection she has at this hour. Detective Sarah Chen, the detective who covers overnight incidents at the museum, could act on this evidence fast—but speed without context has buried investigations before, including yours.

### Choices
1. Confront Hartwell directly with the vault records -> 7
2. Find Detective Chen and hand her the evidence for rapid action -> 6

## Chapter 5
### Title
Ledger of Contradictions

### Content
Director Hartwell's office is unlocked, the door ajar, the desk lamp still warm. The room smells of cold coffee and old paper. On the desk, a red-marked emergency ledger sits open to a page stamped with a handwritten warning: "Do not unseal the moon tablet without full council approval." The same warning appears on six consecutive pages in six different hands. Someone wanted that instruction impossible to miss tonight.

A desk phone sits off its cradle, call log still active. You lift it carefully and hear the tail end of a muffled conversation: someone in law enforcement—voice clipped, professional, using departmental shorthand—discussing convoy routes with a fluency that goes beyond a single confidential tip. The leak is not a one-time betrayal. It is infrastructure, maintained across months.

You set the phone down. The ledger shows Hartwell tried to prevent exactly what happened tonight—the moon tablet's removal was unauthorized by her own standing orders. The phone call shows someone inside law enforcement has been feeding operational details to the other side. One piece of evidence exonerates a suspect. The other implicates an ally. Both are dangerous, and neither is safe to sit on.

Detective Sarah Chen—the detective assigned to overnight museum incidents—has the authority to act on either piece. Director Hartwell has the institutional knowledge to contextualize both. Neither knows yet what you've just learned about the other.

### Choices
1. Go to Hartwell, share what you've found, and plan a quiet strategy -> 7
2. Bring everything to Detective Chen and trust her authority -> 6

## Chapter 6
### Title
Operation Nightglass

### Content
Detective Sarah Chen—the detective handling tonight's museum case—launches an immediate intercept of Director Hartwell's convoy. She doesn't wait for the evidence to be fully catalogued. Her team hits the river bridge at 03:40 AM with lights and authority and the certainty of people who believe they've already won.

The bridge erupts. Masked agents—the Midnight Collective, a third faction allied with neither Chen's unit nor Hartwell's escort—ambush both teams from positions they could only have occupied if they knew the intercept route in advance. The tablet changes hands in under ninety seconds. The Collective's van drops into the flood tunnels beneath the bridge and disappears into the dark.

You stand at the tunnel entrance, night air sharp with cordite and river damp. Chen is beside you, jaw locked, weapon still drawn. Her operation didn't just fail—it was anticipated, every position mapped before her team arrived. Either her unit was compromised from inside, or this intercept was designed from the start to flush the tablet into those tunnels.

Below, the drainage system branches into miles of unlit passages. The van's engine echoes off concrete somewhere ahead, distorted and fading. Director Hartwell—the museum's director—is twenty minutes out with a vetted recovery team: slower, but moving on channels tonight's ambush didn't reach. The tunnel trail is going cold. Everything above ground is blown.

### Choices
1. Pursue the Collective into the tunnels now, before the trail goes dead -> 8
2. Pull back and coordinate with Hartwell's recovery team -> 9

## Chapter 7
### Title
Hartwell's Disclosure

### Content
Director Hartwell doesn't wait for your accusation. She reaches into her desk, pulls a locked case from the bottom drawer, and lays out the network herself: a quiet, cross-institutional operation that identifies and secures anomalous artifacts before trafficking rings reach them. Twelve museums. Three federal agencies. No public mandate, no public record, because the artifacts in question do things that existing policy frameworks cannot classify or contain.

The moon tablet is a locator key. With its embedded safeguards intact, it blocks black-market recovery of cached artifacts worldwide—every hidden vault in the network stays hidden. Without those safeguards, it becomes a map: every site exposed to anyone holding the tablet. Tonight's theft wasn't about selling one artifact. It was about cracking open every secured location simultaneously.

She reads your evidence, sets it down, and meets your eyes. "You've done the hard part," she says. "Now choose what happens next." She is asking you to trust a system that operated in secret—a system that let you take the fall two months ago rather than risk exposing a larger mission. The justification is real. The cost was yours, and no one asked permission before spending it.

Her recovery team can move on the shipping dock within the hour and intercept the tablet before it leaves the city. Or you can take everything you know public—every file, every name, every hidden site—and force accountability the way institutions fear most: with light.

### Choices
1. Join Hartwell's controlled recovery operation -> 9
2. Release all records to the press and force public accountability -> 10

## Chapter 8
### Title
Tunnel Collapse

### Content
The flood tunnels narrow fast. Your flashlight catches slick concrete, standing water the color of rust, and the Collective van's taillights disappearing around a bend thirty meters ahead. You sprint. Your boots echo off the walls and swallow every other sound until the only warning you get is a low mechanical click from somewhere above.

The first charge detonates at the second junction. The ceiling drops in a cascade of fractured concrete and black water, and your radio dies on impact—smashed between your shoulder and the tunnel wall. Something structural gives way behind you, a deep groaning collapse that means the passage you came through no longer exists.

Water rises fast. It reaches your shins in ten seconds and your chest in under a minute, cold enough to lock your muscles. In the dark, the current pulls with a patience that has nothing to do with you personally. The last voice you hear over anything electronic is calm, almost satisfied: "The guard rushed exactly where we needed."

You spend your final moments transmitting your evidence cache—photographs, timestamps, the vault transfer log—upstream through the emergency beacon before the water reaches the equipment. The upload bar fills four seconds before the signal drowns. By dawn the tablet is gone, the Collective has vanished, and your investigation is over. The evidence you sent will outlast you. Whether anyone reads it in time is a question you no longer get to answer.

The End

## Chapter 9
### Title
Recovered Dawn

### Content
Director Hartwell's vetted team stages the intercept at the shipping yard without advance noise—no radio chatter, no police channels, no digital trail for compromised contacts to follow. The operation is surgical. The moon tablet is recovered from a foam-lined case in the hold of a converted fishing vessel, its safeguards intact. Key Collective brokers are arrested at the dock before they reach the auction, their expressions shifting from confidence to confusion as handcuffs close around rehearsed calm.

Detective Chen's compromised contacts unravel through the evidence chain you built—not fast, but clean, each link verified before the next arrest. Three officers. One federal liaison. A network that explains how information moved through the system and why your investigation two months ago was buried before it reached anyone honest enough to read it.

The museum reopens under transparent oversight three weeks later. Your name is cleared in full, in writing, with an apology on the same letterhead that issued your suspension. You read it twice. It does not undo the silence—the empty apartment, the interviews where people studied you like a liability—but it is real, and it is on the record, and it is enough.

You walk the Egyptian wing on your first shift back. The sarcophagus lid is closed, sealed, roped off for restoration. You rest your hand on the velvet barrier and feel the strange, specific calm of someone who knows exactly what is underneath and has chosen to leave it there.

### Choices
The End

## Chapter 10
### Title
Truth Without Guardrails

### Content
You release every file to three journalists before dawn—the vault records, the transfer logs, the shipping manifests, the cross-institutional network Director Hartwell spent years building in silence. The story breaks like a window: fast, loud, and impossible to reassemble.

For one week you are celebrated. The whistleblower. The honest guard. The person who refused to let institutions bury the truth behind classified stamps and quiet internal reviews. Editorials use your name alongside words like integrity and courage. The coverage is accurate. The response is not controlled.

Copycat rings and private militias move on the published site data within ten days, faster than any enforcement body can respond. Cache locations that Hartwell's network spent a decade securing are exposed to anyone with a browser and a motive. Twelve vaults are raided before the end of the month. Artifacts that were contained and dormant are now in circulation—sold, weaponized, or simply lost in transit to buyers who will never surface.

You keep your conscience intact. You told the truth and you meant to help, and both of those things remain true on the mornings when the news is difficult. But the map you drew was read by people you did not account for, and the consequences carry a momentum that no longer answers to your intentions. Some nights you sit with the museum floor plan and trace the path from the open sarcophagus to this moment, step by quiet step, looking for the turn you might have taken differently. You haven't found it yet.

### Choices
The End
