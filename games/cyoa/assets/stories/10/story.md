# Fuel Stop

## Keywords
- entrapment
- courage
- strangers

## Chapter 1
### Title
Two A.M. Fill-Up

### Content
The pump nozzle clicks three times before the gas starts flowing. Something about the altitude, maybe, or the cold — the kind of desert cold that settles into sheet metal and makes everything sound brittle. Your breath fogs under the single fluorescent canopy light. Beyond it, nothing. No highway glow, no distant houses. Just the black line where the road dissolves into scrub and the scrub dissolves into sky.

You didn't plan to stop here. The GPS lost signal forty miles back and this place appeared like a rumor — a single-pump station with a flat-roofed building the color of old teeth, windows lit from within by buzzing tubes. A sign by the road reads OPEN 24 HRS in letters repainted so many times the O is three shades of white.

You lean against the car and check your phone. One bar flickers, dies. The missed call is still there on the lock screen — the same number you've been ignoring for six days. You put the phone away.

Inside the station, through a glass door smudged with handprints at hip-height — small handprints — a woman stands behind the register. Mid-thirties, dark hair pulled tight, apron over a flannel shirt. She watches you with the particular stillness of someone who has been watching for a long time before you noticed.

Behind her, taped to the wall between a cigarette ad and a calendar two years out of date, a child's drawing in crayon: a yellow house with no doors. Just walls and a roof and four windows like closed eyes. The kind of thing you'd glance at and forget.

A pickup truck — big, black, mud on the wheel wells — sits at an angle behind the building, its rear bumper nearly flush with the back exit. Careless parking, you think. Rural indifference.

You push inside. The door chime is a single electronic tone, too loud for the silence it breaks. The air smells like coffee left on the burner since yesterday and something underneath — Pine-Sol, the kind of clean that's working hard to cover something.

The woman — her nametag reads DELIA — meets your eyes and smiles. It's a real smile, but it doesn't reach past her mouth. Her hands arrange a stack of napkins on the counter with the precision of someone defusing a device.

"Heading far?" she asks.

"Albuquerque. Maybe another three hours."

"Anyone waiting up for you?"

You almost answer honestly. "No."

She nods like you've confirmed something. In the window behind you, sun-bleached to near-invisibility, a HELP WANTED sign curls at the edges. You wouldn't notice it unless the light caught it right.

Delia slides something across the counter. A receipt, you think, until you see the handwriting. Block letters, ballpoint, pressed hard enough to dent the paper:

**DON'T LEAVE YET.**

Your eyes come up. Her expression hasn't changed. But her hand, still flat on the counter, is trembling.

### Choices
1. Read the note and ask what this is about -> 2
2. Set a twenty on the counter and walk out -> 3

## Chapter 2
### Title
Delia's Plan

### Content
You turn the note over. Nothing on the back. You look at Delia, and she looks at the ceiling — no, past it. You follow her gaze and find a small security camera mounted in the corner, its red indicator light pulsing steadily. She looks at you, then at the camera, then at you again. The message is clear: not here.

"Restroom's around back," she says, louder than necessary. "Key's on the hook by the door."

The restroom key is attached to a wooden paddle the size of a ruler, scratched and oil-stained. The restroom is outside, around the corner, out of the camera's line of sight. Delia meets you there forty seconds later, stepping through a side door you hadn't noticed. She pulls it shut without letting it click.

In the dark, her composure breaks just enough for you to see what's underneath. Not panic — something older and more controlled. The fixed attention of someone who has been running calculations for a long time and arrived at a single remaining option.

"He owns this station," she says. "He owns the house up the road. He owns the truck. He doesn't own me, but try explaining that to the nearest sheriff forty miles in any direction with no phone and no car."

She doesn't say his name. Just *he.* *Him.* As if naming him might make him appear.

"His name is Wade Prescott. Asleep in his house, a mile up that road." She points north into the dark. "He checks the camera feed from his phone. Not on a schedule — when he can't sleep, when he gets a feeling. If everything looks normal, he rolls over. If it doesn't, he drives down. Seven minutes. I've timed it."

The camera records to a DVR in the supply closet — a six-hour loop, overwriting itself continuously. Whatever it captures now stays on the drive until roughly 8 a.m.

Then the part she's been building toward. She says it without flinching.

"There's a child. Milagro. Five years old. Asleep in the back room."

She watches your face, cataloging your reaction the way she catalogued your answers earlier — *heading far, anyone waiting, how much gas.*

"Milagro is mine. And we're both leaving tonight. But a child changes the math. I can carry a bag and move fast or I can carry a five-year-old and move slow. I can't do both. And that truck" — she tilts her head toward the black pickup blocking the back exit — "is parked there every night. Same angle. Same spot. It's not careless. It's a wall."

No ID. No money. No phone. The nearest landline is in Wade's house. She's been planning for weeks, and the plan has one hole she can't close: she needs a second person with a vehicle.

"I don't need a hero," she says. "I need a car and a second pair of hands."

Camera first means disabling the DVR so Wade sees nothing unusual if he checks — safer, but slower. Going straight for the child means speed, but the camera will record everything you do. If Wade wakes and checks the feed before 8 a.m., he'll see it all.

### Choices
1. Disable the security camera first -> 4
2. Go straight for the child while Wade sleeps -> 5

## Chapter 3
### Title
Eleven Miles South

### Content
You set a twenty on the counter. Delia watches you do it. Her hand — the one that slid you the note — is still flat on the surface, still trembling. She pulls it back. Slowly, like reeling in a line that didn't catch.

"Keep the change," you say, because saying nothing would mean looking at her for one more second, and you can already feel the weight of what you're choosing not to do settling into the architecture of your chest.

The door chime sounds behind you. The night air hits your face. Your car is right there, ten feet away, gas tank full, engine ready to carry you south. You get in. The headlights sweep across the lot — the pumps, the building, the front window where Delia stands behind the register. She's watching you. Her hands are at her sides. She doesn't wave. She doesn't bang on the glass. She doesn't mouth *please.* She just stands there, and you understand that she expected this. She planned for the version where you stayed, but she was prepared for the version where you didn't. She has been prepared for people to leave for a long time.

You pull onto the highway heading south. The station shrinks in the rearview — canopy light, building, the HELP WANTED sign — growing smaller until it's a white speck and then nothing.

You drive eleven miles before the shaking starts. Your hands first, then your arms, then the muscles across your chest. You pull over because you can't hold the wheel steady. The shoulder is gravel and silence. You sit with the engine running and your forehead on your knuckles.

The child's drawing. Yellow house, no doors. You saw it for two seconds and it is etched on the inside of your skull. You will see it when you close your eyes tonight and next week and in six months when you catch the smell of Pine-Sol in a gas station restroom and your heart rate doubles for reasons you will tell no one about.

Your phone finds signal. Two bars. The missed call is still there — the same number, six days running. Someone who needed you once and got silence instead.

The phone glows in the dark. You could call 911 — describe the station, the woman, the note. You could do from eleven miles away what you couldn't do from eight feet. Or you could turn the car around. Eleven miles is twenty minutes. Delia is still behind the counter. The child is still sleeping behind the bolt. The man on the hill is still in his bed.

### Choices
1. Turn the car around and drive back -> 5
2. Call 911 from the shoulder -> 8

## Chapter 4
### Title
The Blind Spot

### Content
"Camera first," you say.

Delia nods — not agreement, exactly, but confirmation. This was her preferred sequence. She leads you back inside and through a door behind the register marked EMPLOYEES ONLY, into a supply closet that smells like motor oil and cardboard. A metal shelf holds windshield-wiper fluid and off-brand antifreeze. On the floor, between a mop bucket and a stack of inventory binders, sits the DVR unit: a beige box the size of a shoebox, one steady green light, warm to the touch.

"Loop resets every six hours," Delia whispers. "The cable from the camera goes in the back."

You crouch behind the unit and feel for the cable. Your fingers brush the wall first — and something taped there, low, where an adult would never look but a child could reach. You pull your phone out and use the flashlight.

A drawing. Crayon on lined paper. Two small stick figures — one with dark hair, one half the size — stand next to a third figure twice as tall as either of them. The tall figure has red hands. Not gloves, not mittens. Hands colored in solid red, the crayon pressed so hard it left wax ridges on the paper.

Delia sees you looking. She says nothing. The silence is its own answer.

You find the cable — a single coaxial line — and disconnect it from the DVR. The green light stays on; the unit still runs, but now it records nothing. If Wade checks his phone, he'll see static or a frozen frame. Either one is suspicious if he's paying attention.

"How long before he notices?"

"Depends on whether he sleeps through. If he checks and sees a dead feed, he'll think glitch first. Maybe ten minutes before he drives down."

You replace the cable loosely — enough that a casual glance wouldn't catch the tampering. Delia positions the mop bucket to obscure the back panel.

The hallway behind the supply closet is narrow, unlit, and smells like a place where the air doesn't move. The door at the end has a bolt lock mounted at adult height — on the outside. Delia slides it back with her thumb, a practiced motion that makes no sound. She's oiled this. Recently.

The room is eight feet by eight feet. Bare mattress, no bed frame. A plastic bin of clothes. Coloring books with cracked spines. No window. On the wall above the mattress, dozens of drawings, overlapping, taped and re-taped. Houses. All houses. Some have doors, some don't.

Delia kneels. Places her hand on the small back. The child — Milagro — opens their eyes without surprise and reaches for her, arms up. On Delia's hip, the child looks at you. Measuring. A five-year-old who has learned to measure people.

"That truck," you say. "The one blocking the back exit. How long?"

"Every night for two years. Since I tried to walk out the first time."

A fire extinguisher hangs on the wall, inspection tag two years expired. A heavy flashlight sits on the shelf. Small advantages. But the clock is what matters.

### Choices
1. Slip out the back and deal with the truck — the dead camera buys you time -> 6
2. Go out the front to your car — the camera's dead, but the canopy light isn't -> 7

## Chapter 5
### Title
No Time to Be Careful

### Content
You move through the station's back corridor. The fluorescent light flickers on a dying ballast, strobing the walls in pale jitters. Every few seconds: full dark, then blue-white, then dark again. In the strobes you catch details — a fire extinguisher with an expired inspection tag, a water stain on the ceiling shaped like a hand, a supply closet door ajar with a DVR's green light pulsing inside.

The security camera is live. Its red indicator is steady and unblinking. It sees the empty counter. It sees the door you walked through. If Wade Prescott wakes up and checks his phone, he will see that the station is unattended at 2:30 a.m. That might mean nothing. That might mean everything.

Delia keeps glancing back toward the camera's glow, her body half-turned. A reflex worn into her nervous system. "He checks when he can't sleep," she says. "And he doesn't sleep well."

The bolt on the child's door is heavy brass, mounted at adult height — on the outside. You slide it back. The metal is cold and the action is loud in the hallway, a hard clack that bounces off the cinderblock walls. Delia winces. You both stand motionless for five seconds, listening. Nothing from outside. No engine starting up the hill.

Inside: a small room, no window. Bare mattress on the floor. Plastic bin of clothes. Coloring books with cracked spines. The overhead bulb has no switch inside the room — the toggle is in the hallway, next to the bolt. On the wall above the mattress, dozens of drawings — houses, all houses. Some have doors, some don't. The yellow one from behind the register is the final version, and now you understand it isn't a child's simplification. It's a child's precision. They drew what they know.

Milagro sits up at the sound of the bolt and looks past you to Delia. No crying, no confusion. The child has been woken by the bolt before and knows what the sound means. Delia scoops Milagro up. The child locks their arms around her neck. Dark eyes find yours over Delia's shoulder. Measuring. A five-year-old who has learned to measure people.

"That truck," you say. "The one blocking the back exit."

"Every night for two years. Magnetic key box under the left rear wheel well." Delia shifts Milagro's weight on her hip and checks the clock on the wall — 2:34 a.m. "We have to move."

### Choices
1. Head to the back exit — the camera can't see you there, but the truck blocks the way -> 6
2. Go out the front to your car — faster, but the camera catches every step -> 7

## Chapter 6
### Title
What's Under the Tarp

### Content
The three of you stand at the back door of the station. The air is cold and still. No lights except the stars and the faint glow of the canopy around the front. Delia holds Milagro on her hip. The child is awake, watchful, eyes reflecting starlight.

The black pickup is right there. Three feet away, rear bumper flush with the exit. A wall on wheels.

"Keys," you say.

"Magnetic box. Under the left rear wheel well. He keeps his house keys in it too — doesn't like carrying a ring. I've known about it for four months."

You kneel by the left rear wheel and feel along the inner lip of the fender. A small metal case, magnetized, cold against the steel. Inside: two keys on a ring. One Ford ignition key. One house key, brass, older.

The truck cab is unlocked. Delia pops the interior light bulb before you sit down — a practiced twist. Behind the station, a dirt track runs south, connecting to the highway two miles out, invisible from Wade's house on the hill.

You turn the key. The engine catches — a low growl that sounds like a shout in this much silence. You pull the truck forward ten feet to clear the exit, then kill the ignition.

Something shifts in the truck bed. Weight sliding on metal. You step out and look over the tailgate. A tarp — olive-green, military surplus — strapped with bungee cords. One corner has come loose.

You lift it.

Your brain catalogs the objects without connecting them: a shovel with clay on the blade. A coil of nylon rope. A woman's shoe — one shoe, white canvas, no pair. A plastic bin with a lid. Three rolls of duct tape.

Then it assembles and the ground tilts.

You think about the child's drawing — the tall figure with the red hands. What a five-year-old sees and cannot name but can draw. You think about Delia saying *he owns this station, he owns the house, he owns the truck,* and what ownership means to a man who keeps a shovel and rope and one woman's shoe under a tarp.

Delia's breathing has changed behind you. She's seen your face.

"How many?" you ask.

"I don't know." Her voice is thin as wire. "There was a woman here before me. Carla. She left one day. That's what he said. She *left.*"

A light flicks on up the hill. Wade's porch light — a yellow smear a mile up the road. He's awake. Maybe the engine woke him. Maybe something he saw — or didn't see — on the camera feed. Either way, the seven-minute clock is running.

A rifle is mounted on a rack behind the truck's front seat. Bolt-action, older model, well-maintained.

Delia says: "He's coming."

Wade is coming on foot — the truck is here. Walking down means he doesn't know the full picture. You have four minutes.

### Choices
1. Run for your car out front -> 7
2. Stand your ground and bluff Wade -> 10

## Chapter 7
### Title
Headlights Behind You

### Content
You move fast. Delia carries Milagro and you lead, cutting around the building toward the front lot. The canopy light turns everything the color of old bone. Above the register door, the security camera's red eye stares down — dead or still feeding Wade's phone, it doesn't matter now. Your car sits at the pump — sixty feet of open ground, every inch lit.

You cross it at a run. Delia's breathing is controlled, the practiced breath of someone who has imagined this sprint a hundred times. Milagro's face is pressed into her shoulder, eyes squeezed shut.

Car doors open. Milagro in the back seat. Delia after. Engine catches. South.

For ninety seconds, the road is empty. The station shrinks in the rearview — canopy light, building, the faded HELP WANTED sign. You start to breathe.

Then: headlights. Coming down the hill road from the north, fast, bouncing across ruts. The black pickup. Wade is awake. He's coming.

The truck is faster than your car. The headlights grow in the mirror. On this straight desert highway there is nowhere to turn, nowhere to hide.

Delia's hand finds the back of your headrest and grips. "He won't stop," she says. "He can't let a witness leave. Not with us in the car."

Then she unbuckles. Leans forward. Speaks into your ear, flat and certain.

"Pull over. Let me out. Then drive."

The headlights cast shadows inside the car. Milagro whimpers — a compressed, airless sound, the sound of a child who learned early that volume brings consequences.

"He wants me," Delia says. "If I'm on the road and you're gone, he'll stop for me. I'm the asset. You're the variable."

"I don't need a hero," she said an hour ago. "I need a car and a second pair of hands." You heard *I need help getting us all out.* But that's not what she said. The car was yours. The hands were yours. She was never counting herself in the escape.

Delia opens the door before you've fully stopped. The wind tears at her hair. She kneels at the open door, takes Milagro's face in both hands, presses her forehead to the child's.

One word. "Go." Not to the child. To you.

She stands and walks toward the approaching headlights. Not running. Walking. Toward the thing she fears most, so a child can leave.

Wade's truck slows. The headlights wash over Delia — a silhouette on the center line, arms at her sides.

Milagro's hand uncurls. A piece of paper, folded small, gripped since the back room. A drawing. Yellow house. No doors. The child holds it out to you. *This is where I lived.*

You drive. In the mirror, two figures on a dark road. One large, one small. The small one isn't fighting.

You will call 911 when the signal returns. You will tell them everything. But you watched a woman walk toward what she feared most so a child could leave, and you drove. Delia made the choice, not you. Except you were in the car and she wasn't.

## Chapter 8
### Title
The Voice on the Line

### Content
You dial 911. The signal wavers — two bars, one, two — but the call connects.

"911, what's your emergency?"

"There's a woman. At a gas station. She slid me a note — she needs help. There's a child."

"Can you give me the location?"

You try. A highway number you half-remember. A direction — north, maybe forty miles from the last town. A single-pump station with one canopy light. The dispatcher types. You hear the keystrokes through the static. She asks for details and each one sounds thinner than the last. A woman behind a counter. A note you didn't keep. A child you didn't see. A truck parked at an angle behind the building.

"And where are you now?"

"On the shoulder. Eleven miles south."

"You left the location?"

The question isn't an accusation. But it lands like one. You left. You are eleven miles away, on a phone, describing a place where a woman is standing behind a register with her hands at her sides.

The dispatcher asks for a name. You give Delia's. She asks for the man's name — you don't have it. Delia didn't say it while you were inside, only *he* and *him,* as if naming him might summon him. The dispatcher says they'll send a unit but it could be over an hour given the location. She asks you to stay on the line.

You sit on the gravel shoulder and stare north. The highway is a straight line in both directions. Nothing but dark and distance and the road back to a gas station where a woman oiled a bolt lock and taped a HELP WANTED sign in a window and waited.

The dispatcher is talking — protocol, your name, a callback number. But part of your mind is doing different math. Eleven miles at seventy is nine minutes. The woman is still behind the counter. The child is still behind the bolt. An hour from now, when the cruiser arrives — if they find the right station, if they find it at all — everything might be different.

You think about the missed call on your lock screen. Six days of someone reaching for you and getting nothing back. You have spent your whole life being the person who leaves.

The engine idles. North is nine minutes. South is the rest of a life defined by phone calls made from a safe distance. Your hands rest on the gearshift, caught between two versions of yourself — the one who reports and the one who returns.

### Choices
1. Tell the dispatcher you're going back — then drive north -> 6
2. Stay on the line and wait for the police -> 9

## Chapter 9
### Title
The Drive Home

### Content
You stay on the line. The dispatcher's voice is calm and procedural and absolutely inadequate for what is happening forty miles north, but it is a voice, and it says the right things: a unit is being dispatched, you've done the right thing, stay where you are.

You stay. You sit in the car on the gravel shoulder with the engine running and the heater on and the phone pressed to your ear. The dispatcher asks follow-up questions — building color, pump count, was the woman injured — and you answer like a witness debriefing, which is what you are. A witness. Someone who saw and reported and remained at a safe distance while the machinery of response grinds into motion.

The call ends after nineteen minutes. The dispatcher thanks you and gives you a case number. You write it on the back of a gas receipt. The numbers look small.

You pull back onto the highway heading south. The road opens ahead — straight, empty, stars you can't name. The car is warm. The tank is full. You are safe. You did something. You told someone. You participated in the system designed to handle this.

Forty miles later, on the outskirts of Albuquerque, you stop at a gas station — a regular one, lit up, other cars, a man inside pouring coffee. You use the restroom. The smell of Pine-Sol hits you and your hands start shaking again.

You grip the sink. In the mirror, the face looking back is the face of someone who called 911 and drove away. Not the face of someone who did nothing — you can point to the case number, the nineteen minutes on the phone — but the face of someone who calculated the distance between helping and being present and chose the version that kept the engine running.

The missed call lights up your phone again. The same number. Six days. Someone reaching for you because something broke and you were the person they thought might help.

You don't answer. You realize that calling 911 from the shoulder was the same gesture you've perfected your whole life: the appearance of response without the cost of return. You phoned it in. Literally. You phoned in a woman's life from the side of the road and drove to a city where no one will ask about a gas station or a child's drawing of a yellow house with no doors.

The case number is in your pocket. A unit was dispatched. Maybe they found her. Maybe they found the child. You'll never know, because knowing would mean going back.

The End

## Chapter 10
### Title
Dawn

### Content
You step forward so your body is between the truck bed and the path Wade is walking down. The rifle is in the truck cab, ten feet behind you. You leave it there. What you need isn't a weapon. What you need is three minutes.

Wade Prescott comes around the corner on foot, no flashlight, navigating by the canopy light. Tall, unhurried, denim jacket over a white undershirt, boots unlaced. His face is unremarkable — thinning hair, sun-lined forehead. What's wrong is the stillness. He moves the way Delia moves, but his version of control is the original and hers is the adaptation.

He sees you. Then the truck pulled forward. Then Delia at the back exit with Milagro. His expression doesn't change. He processes it all and arrives at: manageable.

"Hell of a time for a breakdown," he says.

"Stop there," you say. He stops — not because you're threatening, but because your calm confuses him.

"I looked in the truck," you say.

Wade's face goes through something — not a change of expression but a subtraction of it. Everything human drains out.

"I called the police twenty minutes ago. When I pulled in and still had signal. I gave them the address and the name Wade Prescott and told them a woman and her child need help."

It's a lie. Your phone has been dead since you arrived. But Wade doesn't know when you got here. He doesn't know the desert's signal map. The phone signal is a variable he can't check without going up the hill.

You hold his gaze. The shoe with no pair. The clay on the shovel. You don't have to manufacture conviction.

"You're bluffing," Wade says. But he doesn't move.

"The shovel has clay on it. One woman's shoe. Three rolls of duct tape. You want to explain that to me, or to whoever's on their way?"

His hands open and close once. He looks at the truck, the tarp, the highway south.

"Delia," he says. "Tell this person—"

"No." Delia's voice comes from behind you, clear for the first time tonight. Not whispering. Not watching the camera. Using the full register of a voice she hasn't raised in two years. "No."

You back toward your car, keeping your body between Wade and Delia's path. She moves fast — Milagro in her arms, a bag packed weeks ago over one shoulder. Car doors open and close.

Wade stands in the canopy light, running the math. You pull south. In the mirror he's still there — not chasing, because the bluff holds.

Nobody speaks for twenty minutes. Milagro falls asleep, head on Delia's lap, one hand gripping a drawing — yellow house, no doors.

Your phone finds signal at mile twenty-two. You call 911. The address, the name, the truck, the contents. The dispatcher asks if you're safe and you say yes, understanding for the first time that the word is not about you.

Delia speaks without turning from the window. "I put that sign up the week after he took my phone. The HELP WANTED sign. I thought someone would come." She pauses. "You came."

A gas station on the edge of town — regular, lit up, other cars. Delia stands in the parking lot holding Milagro, breathing air that doesn't belong to anyone. The child wakes, looks around at the lights, and says the first word you've heard them speak: "More."

Your phone buzzes. The missed call — six days. You call back. Someone answers.

The End
