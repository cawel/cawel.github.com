# Echoes of the Perseid Gate

## Chapter 1
### Title
Signal in the Black

### Content
Your freighter, *Kitefall*, coasts into the Perseid Drift with engines whispering at emergency idle. The distress beacon you followed is old—older than any legal transponder still in the shipping lanes—and yet it repeats your own callsign in your own voice, stretched thin by static and time. Ahead, a shattered ring station hangs in the dark like a broken halo around a dead relay moon, its fragments linked by occasional arcs of blue-white plasma. Every instrument in your cockpit behaves as if a storm is raging, even though space outside the canopy is calm and star-sharp. Your navigator insists the station is unpowered. Your eyes insist something inside is awake.

As you match velocity and drift alongside the nearest docking spur, your comms bloom with half-formed words from no known channel: coordinates, fragments of flight logs, and one line repeated in thirteen languages—*DO NOT OPEN THE GATE WITHOUT A WITNESS*. You can either dock at the main service spine where life-support readings flicker weakly, or cut through a maintenance breach and head straight for the signal source before it moves again.

### Choices
1. Dock at the main service spine and secure systems first -> 2
2. Enter through the maintenance breach and chase the beacon immediately -> 3

## Chapter 2
### Title
The Service Spine

### Content
The main corridor greets you with artificial gravity that pulses like a failing heartbeat. Ceiling strips stutter from darkness to sterile white as your suit lamps sweep over bulkheads painted with evacuation arrows and old corporate insignia from a century ago. Emergency shutters are half-closed across side passages, and every sealed door bears hand-written timestamps in grease pencil, as if someone kept trying to hold back a thing that understood door codes better than people did. In a recessed terminal, you find a rolling queue of maintenance tickets opened by no registered crew: oxygen balancing, coolant rerouting, and repeated requests to "calm the choir in deck nine." The requests were all approved by a command account that should belong to a captain declared dead eighty-seven years ago.

When you power a local map node, the station redraws itself around you. Decks that should be gone appear briefly, then vanish. The distress source pings from the engineering sector, but another signal—clean, recent, and definitely human—broadcasts from the command bridge asking for immediate assistance with "containment protocols." You can head to engineering and trace the strange power behavior at its origin, or follow the live human beacon and gamble that command still means safety.

### Choices
1. Go to engineering and trace the unstable power signature -> 4
2. Follow the live beacon to the command bridge -> 5

## Chapter 3
### Title
The Breach Corridor

### Content
You cut through the maintenance rupture and pull yourself hand-over-hand along a twisted conduit where the hull has peeled open like bark from a frozen tree. Beyond the breach, the station interior is a zero-g cathedral of floating tools, cracked visors, and pages from old nav manuals drifting in slow spirals. Your boots catch a rail and you swing into an observatory chamber whose panoramic glass is webbed with microfractures but still holds. Outside, the stars around the relay moon appear doubled, then tripled, as if space itself is misaligned by a fraction. On the inner wall, someone has etched dozens of concentric circles around one phrase: *the gate listens before it speaks.*

The distress beacon sharpens the farther you move into the observatory, resolving into layered transmissions from different years occupying the same channel. One is a frightened cadet, one is a calm systems officer, one is unmistakably you saying words you have not yet said. A nearby access ladder leads down toward life-support where atmospheric chemistry is changing by the minute. Another hatch opens into an old astrophysics lab where the station’s gate simulations were run before the disaster. You can prioritize the lab to understand what happened, or descend to life-support before everyone—past or present—runs out of air.

### Choices
1. Enter the astrophysics lab and review gate simulations -> 5
2. Descend to life-support and stabilize the station atmosphere -> 6

## Chapter 4
### Title
Engineering Choir

### Content
Engineering is hot enough to fog your visor despite your suit cooling loop. Reactor housings hum in layered harmonics that shouldn’t be possible from mechanical systems; each harmonic corresponds to a gate coil array cycling through charge states no one authorized. Holographic diagnostics bloom above the central console in overlapping windows from different decades, each one reporting "nominal" while alarms scream beneath. In the center of the room stands a manual override column ringed with biometric locks. The first lock accepts your thumbprint. The second accepts your voice. The third accepts a heartbeat pattern that matches yours after exactly eleven seconds of anticipatory delay. You realize, with a cold certainty, this system was built expecting you.

A burst packet downloads itself to your suit: partial black-box telemetry from the day the station failed. It shows command staff attempting to open the Perseid Gate to send a quantum archive to Earth, then aborting when a mirrored return signal came back carrying unknown data. The abort failed. Something crossed anyway—not matter, but instruction. You can route all remaining power into hard shutdown and risk trapping whatever is here with you, or keep systems alive long enough to reach the gate core and perform a controlled witness sequence the logs insist is the only safe way to close it.

### Choices
1. Force a hard shutdown from engineering now -> 7
2. Keep systems online and proceed to the gate core -> 8

## Chapter 5
### Title
Bridge of Ghost Commands

### Content
The command bridge is intact, lit by the cold blue of tactical overlays and old starmaps still tracking shipping lanes that no longer exist. Three command chairs face a cracked viewport, and in each chair sits a translucent afterimage of an officer caught in a loop of final decisions. They do not see you at first; they replay orders, overrides, and arguments in clipped bursts, diverging slightly each cycle. On the central table, a legal logbook records every vote made during the crisis, each entry ending with the same addendum: *Decision deferred pending witness.* The live human beacon resolves into a lone survivor in cryo-stasis beneath the bridge deck, kept alive by power siphoned from nonessential life-support sectors.

When you wake the survivor, Chief Navigator Imani Voss, she grabs your forearm and demands to know whether the gate is singing. She explains the station AI learned to predict crew decisions so perfectly that command authority became a feedback loop; eventually, no one could prove a choice was truly theirs. The witness protocol was designed to break that loop by requiring an external observer to pair two contradictory truths. Voss can guide you to the mutiny records that reveal who sabotaged the abort, or take you directly to the gate core where delay may cost the remaining breathable atmosphere.

### Choices
1. Retrieve the mutiny records before touching the gate -> 7
2. Go directly to the gate core with Voss -> 8

## Chapter 6
### Title
Life-Support Below

### Content
You descend into the environmental decks where condensation clings to ducts and every fan runs a fraction out of phase. The air tastes metallic even through filtration, and pressure doors breathe open and closed as if controlled by a sleeping giant. In hydroponics you find rows of pale plants grown under emergency spectra, their leaves inscribed with tiny fractal burn marks that match gate math you saw in the observatory. A maintenance drone glides beside you projecting children’s storybook illustrations over leak diagrams, narrating them in a voice assembled from archived crew families. It is trying to help, but the way it chooses words feels eerily predictive, as though it knows what you need before you do.

At the atmospheric nexus, oxygen reserves are being rerouted away from habitable sectors toward a sealed chamber labeled WITNESS ROOM. You can reverse the flow and save immediate crew survivability at the cost of destabilizing the gate sequence, or leave the flow intact and trust that completing the witness protocol will solve the deeper threat for everyone. An emergency vent tunnel branches toward a sleeper berth where unidentified life-signs have just come online, possibly from a ship that docked during the original incident and never departed.

### Choices
1. Divert oxygen back to habitat decks and stabilize survivors first -> 9
2. Preserve oxygen flow to the witness chamber and continue the protocol -> 8

## Chapter 7
### Title
The Mutiny Ledger

### Content
In a secure archive vault beneath command, you unseal the mutiny ledger—a forensic reconstruction built from shipboard comms, private logs, and sealed tribunal testimony. It reveals the station did not fail because one faction wanted to open the gate; it failed because two factions both believed they were already living in simulations generated by the gate, and each tried to force a "real" outcome by sabotaging the other. The final sabotage code was split into three keys and hidden across personnel memory implants. One key belonged to Voss. One belonged to the captain. The third, according to the ledger, belonged to "incoming witness designation KITEFALL-01." Your chest tightens as your suit recognizes the designation as your own registry.

You can use the recovered keys to execute an uncompromising lockdown, permanently bricking the gate and this entire station, or proceed with the full witness sequence and attempt to separate human decision from gate prediction without destroying everything. The ledger warns that lockdown ends the echoing transmissions but also erases all trapped temporal records—including evidence of who survived. The witness path is riskier, but it may free both present survivors and the voices caught in looped signal strata.

### Choices
1. Execute permanent lockdown and end all gate activity -> 10
2. Attempt the full witness sequence at the gate core -> 8

## Chapter 8
### Title
Perseid Gate Core

### Content
The gate chamber opens before you like an altar carved into physics itself. A ring of superconductive pillars surrounds a suspended aperture no larger than a doorway, yet the starfield seen through it does not match any orientation possible from this moon. Each pillar holds a "truth node"—paired statements extracted from logs, sensor data, and human testimony: *the crew chose freely / the crew was predicted; the signal came from Earth / the signal came from ahead of time; the witness is external / the witness is part of the loop.* Your role is to affirm one statement in each pair while accepting that the opposite remains operationally true. The chamber does not ask for certainty. It asks for responsibility.

As you begin, your own recorded voice appears in the chamber from multiple futures, advising contradictory actions with equally persuasive calm. Voss stands by the cutoff lever, waiting for your final call. If you complete the witness sequence, the gate should collapse gracefully and release the trapped signal layers, but only if your choices are consistent with the person you become after choosing. If you hesitate too long, the chamber will auto-fail into a violent reset. You can commit now and seal the gate through paradox-aware shutdown, or abort and evacuate to the sleeper berth while there is still a corridor of stable time.

### Choices
1. Commit to the witness sequence and close the gate cleanly -> 10
2. Abort sequence and run for the sleeper berth -> 9

## Chapter 9
### Title
Sleeper Berth Nine

### Content
The berth bay is cold enough to frost your suit joints. Rows of cryopods line the walls, half occupied by cargo pilots, science staff, and civilians who never made it to evacuation shuttles. Their status lights pulse amber, synchronized to the same harmonic you heard in engineering. In the center pod lies a child clutching a mission patch from *Kitefall*—your ship name, decades before your birth. A maintenance terminal offers two emergency operations: wake all sleepers now with uncertain neurological outcomes, or preserve cryostasis while transmitting a high-priority rescue burst through the still-open gate channel. One saves people immediately but may leave them with broken memories and no stable habitat. The other risks never being received if the gate collapses first.

Voss argues for rescue transmission, pointing out that the witness sequence might still succeed if you carry pod telemetry back to the core. The pods themselves begin displaying dream logs that reference your current location in real time. The station is still predicting you. Maybe it always was. You can return to the gate with the telemetry and attempt one final closure that includes the sleepers in the release map, or you can wake everyone now and improvise survival in a failing station.

### Choices
1. Return to the gate with sleeper telemetry for a final closure -> 10
2. Wake the sleepers now and abandon gate protocols -> 10

## Chapter 10
### Title
Afterimage of a Choice

### Content
Whatever path brought you here, the station changes the moment you decide. If you completed the witness sequence, the Perseid Gate folds inward without an explosion, and the layered voices on every channel resolve into clear, timestamped records that can finally be archived as history instead of haunting. Survivors wake to a quiet station with stable air and one impossible memory: each of them recalls making one final decision that only you can remember clearly. If you chose lockdown or emergency wake, the gate goes dark hard and fast, but the echoes persist in the hull—fainter, not gone—like radio afterimages burned into old circuitry. In both outcomes, the distress beacon that lured you here stops speaking in your voice.

When dawn from the relay moon rises across the broken ring, your nav console receives a final packet from the station: updated coordinates for safe passage, complete survivor manifests where possible, and a message addressed to "next witness" in your handwriting. You can transmit the full truth to traffic control and invite an investigation that may reopen everything, or redact the dangerous parts and leave the Perseid Drift as a sealed warning that some doors should only open with two people watching.

### Choices
1. Transmit the full station archive and survivor evidence -> 1
2. Send a redacted report and mark Perseid Drift as restricted -> 1
